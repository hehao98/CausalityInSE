\begin{abstract}
\emph{``Correlation does not imply causation''} is among the most widely repeated maxims in empirical software engineering (SE), which is technically correct, but not particularly useful.
The practical questions motivating much empirical SE research are \emph{causal} in nature, concerning the consequences of decisions, interventions, and changes to practice, and for many settings, the studies we can feasibly conduct on these questions are often observational.
Despite the SE community's long engagement with this tension, the field lacks a shared vocabulary for reasoning about when and why a particular study design supports a causal claim versus a purely descriptive one.
Our classification of 5,341 papers at ICSE, FSE, and ASE (2015--2025) quantifies this gap: 29\% of empirical SE papers are motivated by a causal ambition, but only 1.9\% employ a recognized causal inference method.
To address this gap, we argue that the field needs to move forward and adopt the modern causal inference toolkit as the new standard for intervention-outcome questions involving observational SE data.
Importantly, the power of the toolkit lies in making identifying assumptions \emph{transparent and open to scrutiny}, ensuring that the strength of evidence is clearly communicated and that the field can iteratively develop more credible designs.
We demonstrate the full arc of a credible causal inquiry through a worked example on the impact of systematic AI coding tool adoption on development velocity in open-source projects, progressing from na\"ive group comparisons through DAG-guided cross-sectional analysis to a staggered difference-in-differences design.
We envision that the adoption of this toolkit in empirical SE research will bring productive advancements on a broad range of important intervention-outcome questions involving observational SE data.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Data Availability}
\label{sec:data-availability}

All data, analysis scripts, classification prompts, and R~notebooks used in this paper are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/hehao98/CausalityInSE}.
The repository includes the LLM-classified corpus of 5,341 SE papers (Section~\ref{sec:bg-adoption}), the AI adoption dataset of 1,000 GitHub repositories with monthly commit histories (Section~\ref{sec:worked-example}), and all R~Markdown notebooks that reproduce the figures, tables, and statistical analyses reported in this paper.
