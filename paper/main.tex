\begin{abstract}
Empirical software engineering (SE) research frequently investigates causal questions---does adopting a new language reduce defects? does code review improve quality?---yet rarely employs the identification strategies necessary to support causal conclusions.
This disconnect between causal ambition and methodological practice mirrors a well-documented crisis in psychology and health research, but the SE community lacks an accessible, unified introduction to the causal inference toolkit for substantiating causal claims.
This tutorial paper addresses that gap.
We provide a self-contained primer on causal inference for SE researchers, covering the potential outcomes framework, graphical causal models, and design-based identification strategies (difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, panel fixed effects).
Taking the stance that no causal inquiry is perfect by design, we introduce a four-step framework for \emph{assessing} the credibility of causal claims in SE research: (1)~derive an explicit causal theory from SE domain knowledge, (2)~define the causal estimand and specify identifying assumptions for causal interpretation, (3)~honestly acknowledge and mitigate limitations, and (4)~thoughtfully navigate alternative explanations.
We demonstrate the framework's diagnostic and constructive power through two worked examples drawn from the programming-language and code-quality debate.
In the first example, we apply the four steps to diagnose the identification controversy in \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}'s landmark GitHub study and show how panel fixed effects, applied to the same data, yield identification more robust to common unobserved confounders in the SE context.
In the second example, we diagnose the selection bias in Bogner and Merkel's cross-sectional JavaScript vs.\ TypeScript comparison, decompose the compound ``language'' treatment into the sharper ``type system adoption'' question, and redesign the study as a TypeScript migration difference-in-differences.
Both examples illustrate how the framework systematically improves prior research designs.
As a result, we hope this paper will provide a methodological blueprint for future empirical SE research, illustrating how to balance rigor with practicality to generate actionable, trustworthy evidence for important empirical SE questions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Software engineering is among the most consequential engineering disciplines of the modern era, yet its practice is characterized more by folklore, intuition, and contested conventional wisdom than by empirically validated guidance~\cite{DBLP:conf/icse/Devanbu0B16, DBLP:journals/ese/GarousiBO20, DBLP:journals/ese/ShrikanthNFM21, enoiu2026folklore}.
When practitioners ask whether adopting a strongly typed language reduces defects, whether code review improves maintainability, or whether test-driven development increases productivity, they are asking \emph{causal} questions---questions about what would happen if a practice were adopted or abandoned, holding all else constant.
Empirical software engineering research has generated thousands of studies addressing such questions, yet this substantial body of work has largely failed to provide definitive answers.
The problem lies not in the quantity of research, but in its methodological limitations, specifically the field's pervasive reliance on descriptive and correlational evidence from software repository data that cannot distinguish genuine causal effects from selection biases and confounding factors.

The programming language and code quality debate illustrates this crisis.
\citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14, DBLP:journals/cacm/RayPDF17} analyzed millions of commits across thousands of GitHub projects, reporting that language design has a modest but significant correlation on defect proneness.
Although the authors were careful to frame their findings as correlational, many downstream works cited the study as evidence that certain languages \emph{cause} fewer bugs~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894}.
A subsequent reproduction~\cite{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} raised data quality and modeling concerns, prompting the observation that \emph{``an analysis of GitHub is necessarily measuring correlation and not establishing causation, as it does not constitute an experiment''}~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894}---though the two groups continue to disagree on specific measurement choices.
This exchange exposes a deeper methodological impasse: The field's dominant analytical approach---large-scale analysis of observational software-repository data---cannot credibly answer the causal questions that practitioners care about and that motivate a substantial body of empirical SE research.
Even worse, when studies have produced contested results, the field may be buried in unproductive discussions over ``correlation vs. causation'' or whether the findings ``seem valid,'' rather than engaging with the more productive question of \emph{under which specific conditions} the observational data \emph{could} support a causal interpretation. %nor does it articulate what specific confounders or alternative causal structures threaten the findings.

Modern causal inference methods, developed primarily in econometrics and epidemiology over the past several decades, offer a principled path forward~\cite{angrist2010credibility, pearl2009causality, cunningham2021causal}.
The value of these methods lies not in any claim to methodological perfection but in their insistence on \emph{transparency}: Every causal design rests on assumptions that must be explicitly stated, examined, and debated.
When a difference-in-differences study claims a causal effect, it does so under the assumption of parallel trends---an assumption that can be partially tested and openly scrutinized~\cite{roth2023whats}.
When an instrumental variables analysis identifies an effect, it does so under exclusion restrictions that critics can directly challenge~\cite{deaton2010instruments}.
This methodological transparency addresses the core problem: With contested study findings, progress requires not endless reanalysis but a shared framework for evaluating whether findings are plausible given the research context.

However, the SE community lacks an accessible, unified introduction to this toolkit.
Our analysis of 5,341 papers from ICSE, FSE, and ASE (2015--2025) reveals that 29\% of empirical SE papers make causal claims, yet fewer than 4\% employ any recognized causal inference method---and those that do rely overwhelmingly on controlled experiments or causal fault localization rather than the design-based identification strategies suited to the field's observational data (Section~\ref{sec:bg-adoption}).
Recent methodological advances in SE have begun to address specific gaps---instrumental variables~\cite{DBLP:journals/tosem/GrafVlachyW24}, omitted variable bias~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2501-17026}, causal DAGs for requirements engineering~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2511-03875}---but no existing work spans the potential outcomes framework, graphical causal models, and design-based identification strategies, nor offers a systematic protocol for assessing causal credibility.
This tutorial paper fills that gap.

We make three contributions.
First, we provide a self-contained primer on causal inference for SE researchers (Section~\ref{sec:primer}), covering the potential outcomes framework, graphical causal models, and design-based identification strategies using SE-specific examples.
Second, we introduce a four-step causal credibility assessment framework (Section~\ref{sec:framework}) that guides researchers to (1)~derive an explicit causal theory from domain knowledge, (2)~define the causal estimand and specify identifying assumptions, (3)~honestly acknowledge and mitigate limitations, and (4)~thoughtfully navigate alternative explanations.
Third, we demonstrate the framework's diagnostic and constructive power through two worked examples from the programming language debate.
In Example~A (Section~\ref{sec:example-a}), we apply the four steps to \citeauthor{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}'s study, showing how the cross-sectional design limits causal interpretation and how panel fixed effects on the same data strengthen identification by absorbing time-invariant confounders.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Existing Tutorials and Methodological Guides in SE}
\label{sec:bg-tutorials}

The software engineering community has invested substantially in methodological guidance for empirical research---notable efforts include \citeauthor{DBLP:books/sp/WohlinRHORW24}'s \emph{Experimentation in Software Engineering}~\cite{DBLP:books/sp/WohlinRHORW24} textbook, the ACM SIGSOFT Empirical Standards~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2010-03525} that provides 19 method-specific checklists for each major SE research method, and \citeauthor{DBLP:journals/tosem/RobillardAEGLNNSSS24}'s~\cite{DBLP:journals/tosem/RobillardAEGLNNSSS24} trade-off framing for threats to validity discussions.
However, these resources provide general methodological infrastructure for empirical SE---covering how to design, execute, and report studies---rather than addressing the specific problem of how to substantiate causal claims from observational data: e.g., there is no discussion of identification strategies, sensitivity analysis, or criteria for evaluating when correlational evidence can bear a causal interpretation.

The field has gradually begun to address this gap, though contributions remain fragmented.
On the diagnostic side, systematic reviews have documented the state of practice: \citeauthor{DBLP:journals/infsof/Siebert23}'s~\cite{DBLP:journals/infsof/Siebert23} mapping study found only 25 SE papers (2010--2022) applying Pearl's graphical framework, while \citet{DBLP:journals/infsof/GiamatteiGPR25} expanded the scope to 86 papers and revealed that ``causal reasoning'' in SE quality assurance often refers loosely to fault localization heuristics rather than formal causal identification.
A subsequent special issue~\cite{journals/infsof/SiebertTGWKS25} confirmed that causal method use remains ``sporadic,'' and \citet{journals/ese/HulseEM25} raised a cautionary flag by showing that causal graph generators are unstable---with over half the discovered edges changing across minor data perturbations---implicitly strengthening the case for theory-driven DAG construction over data-driven causal discovery.
These diagnostic efforts focus exclusively on Pearl's graphical paradigm, leaving the design-based credibility revolution and the potential outcomes framework unaddressed.

On the prescriptive side, individual tutorials have introduced specific methods for causal inference or for strengthening causal credibility to SE audiences---including a tutorial on instrumental variables~\cite{DBLP:journals/tosem/GrafVlachyW24}, a discussion of strategies for mitigating omitted variable bias~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2501-17026}, and the use of causal DAGs for requirements engineering~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2511-03875}---but each addresses a single method or concern without situating it in the broader causal inference landscape.
Our tutorial fills this gap with an integrative treatment, covering multiple intellectual traditions of causal inference (potential outcomes, graphical causal models, design-based identification, Section~\ref{sec:primer}) and a practical framework for SE research (Section~\ref{sec:framework}).

\subsection{The Causal Inference Challenge in Econometrics, Psychology and Epidemiology Research}
\label{sec:bg-psychology}

\paragraph{Experimental Foundations and Observational Extensions.}
The conceptual foundations were laid when \citet{neyman1923application} introduced potential outcomes notation---the causal effect as the difference between outcomes under treatment and control, of which only one is observed---and \citet{fisher1935design} established randomization as the principled basis for causal attribution.
Together, they established the twin pillars of experimental causal inference, but their frameworks require randomization---a condition rarely met in SE, where programming languages, tools, and processes cannot be randomly assigned to developers or projects.
Extending causal reasoning to observational settings required three advances: \citet{hill1965environment} proposed nine viewpoints for evaluating whether observed associations reflect genuine causal relationships; \citet{rubin1974estimating} formalized \emph{conditional ignorability}, showing that causal effects are identifiable if treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes conditional on observed covariates; and \citet{rosenbaum1983central} introduced the propensity score as a practical dimension-reducing device for covariate adjustment.
These methods share a fundamental limitation: They cannot address confounding from unobserved variables, a constraint that motivated the design-based tradition.

\paragraph{The Design-Based Credibility Revolution.}
The credibility revolution shifted the basis of causal claims from statistical modeling to research design.
\citeauthor{card1994minimum}'s~\cite{card1994minimum} difference-in-differences study of a New Jersey minimum wage increase demonstrated that natural experiments can overturn decades of cross-sectional conventional wisdom; \citet{angrist1996identification} formalized instrumental variable identification through the LATE theorem, providing precise conditions that transformed IV into a principled identification strategy; and \citet{shadish2002experimental} codified threats to quasi-experimental validity.
\citet{angrist2010credibility} crystallized the overarching manifesto: Design-based studies exploiting natural experiments yield more credible causal evidence than advances in statistical modeling alone.
This ethos---deriving credibility from research design rather than modeling assumptions---is foundational to our tutorial and has not been systematically introduced to SE audiences.

\paragraph{Graphical Causal Models.}
Independently, \citet{pearl2009causality} developed the graphical framework, representing causal relationships as DAGs and introducing the \emph{do-calculus} and \emph{back-door criterion} for determining sufficient adjustment sets.
Where the potential outcomes tradition defines \emph{what} to estimate, DAGs provide a visual tool for arguing \emph{why} assumptions hold---making causal assumptions explicit so that critics can challenge specific edges rather than raising vague concerns about ``omitted variables.''
\citet{morgan2015counterfactuals} bridged the two traditions for social science, and \citet{imbens2020potential} clarified their relative strengths, arguing that potential outcomes are more natural for design-based research while DAGs excel at encoding complex causal structures---a synthesis our tutorial brings to SE audiences.

\paragraph{Modern Advances.}
Recent methodological frontiers extend the toolkit in directions relevant to SE\@.
\citet{chernozhukov2018double} introduced double/debiased machine learning for high-dimensional treatment effect estimation, and \citet{athey2016recursive} developed causal forests for heterogeneous effects---both addressing the high-dimensional feature spaces common in mining software repositories.
\citet{callaway2021difference} and \citet{roth2023whats} revealed that standard two-way fixed effects DiD can produce biased estimates under staggered adoption and heterogeneous effects, providing alternative estimators directly relevant to SE panel data studying tool adoption or language switching.
Sensitivity analysis has matured into an essential complement: \citeauthor{rosenbaum2002observational}'s~\cite{rosenbaum2002observational} bounds on hidden bias, \citeauthor{oster2019unobservable}'s~\cite{oster2019unobservable} coefficient stability framework, and \citeauthor{cinelli2020making}'s~\cite{cinelli2020making} robustness value provide tools for assessing vulnerability to unobserved confounding---none yet applied in SE\@.
A new generation of accessible textbooks~\cite{angrist2009mostly, hernan2020causal, cunningham2021causal, huntingtonklein2021effect} has demonstrated that these methods can be taught effectively to non-specialist audiences; \citeauthor{hernan2020causal}'s~\cite{hernan2020causal} \emph{target trial} framework, which asks researchers to describe the hypothetical randomized trial their observational analysis attempts to emulate, is particularly relevant to SE\@.

Psychology and health research have confronted the same disconnect between causal ambition and methodological practice that we identify in SE, and their experience---unfolding in three phases---reveals both consequences and remedies relevant to the SE community.

\paragraph{Diagnosing the Causal Language Problem.}
\citet{hernan2018cword} challenged the epidemiological norm of replacing causal language with associational euphemisms, arguing that this linguistic evasion does not prevent causal interpretation but merely obscures the causal question and prevents transparent discussion of identification assumptions.
\citet{grosz2020taboo} documented a parallel ``taboo'' in psychology, showing that the norm against explicit causal inference pushes causal interpretations underground---resulting in impaired study design, stunted cumulative research, and disconnect between findings and downstream interpretation.
\citet{haber2022causal} provided large-scale empirical evidence that this taboo fails on its own terms: Among 1,170 articles from 18 high-profile health journals, 52\% of abstracts implied moderate or strong causality despite ostensibly associational language.
The SE community exhibits the same pattern, as the language--defect debate (Section~\ref{sec:bg-pl-debate}) illustrates.

\paragraph{Constructive Reforms.}
In response, \citet{rohrer2018thinking} introduced DAGs to psychology as a practical tool for reasoning about confounding, and \citet{wysocki2022statistical} demonstrated through simulation that controlling for mediators, colliders, or their descendants can introduce \emph{more} bias---a finding directly applicable to SE studies that include covariates without DAG-based justification.
\citet{lundberg2021estimand} formalized the \emph{estimand-first} principle---every quantitative study should begin by clearly defining its target quantity outside any particular statistical model---which informs our Step~2.
Institutionally, \citet{lederer2019control} issued editor-endorsed guidance requiring authors to specify causal questions and justify covariate selection---editorial policy that does not yet exist in any SE venue.
\citet{tennant2021dags} assessed DAG adoption in 234 health research articles and found that only 21\% reported target estimands, revealing a theory-practice gap that parallels \citeauthor{DBLP:journals/infsof/Siebert23}'s~\cite{DBLP:journals/infsof/Siebert23} findings in SE\@.

\paragraph{A Cautionary Parallel.}
The income--happiness debate illustrates these consequences: \citet{killingsworth2023income} appeared to resolve a long-standing conflict through sophisticated observational analysis, only for \citet{rohrer2024inappropriate} to demonstrate that the resolution rested on untested identification assumptions---effectively performing Steps~1--4 of our framework on a study that had not performed them.
This sequence mirrors the trajectory from \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14} through \citeauthor{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}'s~\cite{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} reproduction in SE: In both cases, increasingly sophisticated correlational analyses cannot resolve causal questions without explicit identification.
\citeauthor{rohrer2024causal}'s~\cite{rohrer2024causal} recent tutorial for psychologists---demonstrating that causal reasoning is unavoidable even for researchers who work exclusively with observational data---is the closest existing analog to our work, but does not cover design-based identification strategies or SE-specific confounding structures.
The solutions emerging across these disciplines---explicit causal questions, DAG-based covariate justification, estimand-first workflows, sensitivity analysis, and editorial enforcement---can inform and accelerate SE's methodological reform.

\subsection{Causal Inference Adoption in Software Engineering to Date}
\label{sec:bg-adoption}

The preceding sections document a growing awareness of causal reasoning in SE, but how widespread is its actual use?
To quantify the gap between causal ambition and methodological practice, we conducted a systematic classification of all main-research-track papers published at three premier SE venues---ICSE, FSE, and ASE---from 2015 to 2025.

\paragraph{Corpus and Classification.}
Paper titles and DOIs were obtained from DBLP proceedings pages; abstracts were retrieved via the Semantic Scholar batch API with OpenAlex as a backfill source.
This produced a corpus of 5,341 papers.
Each paper was classified by a large language model (Claude Opus 4.6, \texttt{temperature\,=\,0}, structured JSON output) along three dimensions: (1)~whether the paper is an empirical SE study, (2)~whether it is a mining software repositories (MSR) study, and (3)~whether it makes causal claims, employs a causal inference method, or both.
For papers tagged as using causal methods, a follow-up pass assigned each paper to one or more categories in a 13-category taxonomy (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}).
Deterministic decoding and an explicit classification schema were chosen to maximize reproducibility; all prompts, scripts, and classified data are available in the replication package.

\paragraph{The Causal Ambition--Methodology Gap.}
Of the 5,341 papers, 2,136 (40\%) are empirical SE studies.
Among these empirical papers, 621 (29\%) make causal claims---asserting that one variable ``affects,'' ``causes,'' ``leads to,'' or ``increases'' another---yet only 81 (3.8\%) employ any recognizable causal inference method.
The remaining 545 papers make causal claims supported by correlational or descriptive evidence alone.
Figure~\ref{fig:causal-trends} plots this gap over time: While the absolute number of papers making causal claims has grown steadily---from 23 in 2015 to 131 in 2025---the number employing causal methods has grown far more slowly, from 3 to 14 over the same period.
The ratio of causal claims to causal methods has remained roughly 7-to-1 throughout the decade, indicating that the gap is structural rather than transitional.

The gap is particularly pronounced among MSR papers.
Of the 961 MSR papers in our corpus, 249 (26\%) make causal claims, but only 30 (3.1\%) employ causal methods---consistent with the reliance on large-scale observational data that cannot distinguish causal effects from confounding without an identification strategy.

\paragraph{Which Causal Methods Are Used?}
Among the 100 papers that employ causal methods, the distribution is strikingly concentrated (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}).
Randomized controlled experiments dominate (37 papers), reflecting the SE community's established tradition of controlled developer studies~\cite{DBLP:books/sp/WohlinRHORW24}.
Causal fault localization---applying causal testing, causal contribution scores, or causal debugging to identify root causes of failures---accounts for 17 papers, representing a growing research thread that applies causal reasoning to software analysis rather than to empirical research methodology.
Regression-based causal inference (11 papers) includes studies that use regression or observational analysis with explicit causal intent, such as controlling for confounders or using causal-adjacent language, but without formal identification strategies.

The methods central to the credibility revolution in economics and political science are nearly absent.
Only 7 papers employ quasi-experimental or natural experiment designs; propensity score methods appear in just 1 paper; and no paper in our corpus uses instrumental variables, difference-in-differences, or regression discontinuity as a primary identification strategy.
The graphical causal model tradition is better represented (7 papers using causal DAGs, 3 using causal discovery algorithms, 4 using interventional or do-calculus reasoning), consistent with \citeauthor{DBLP:journals/infsof/Siebert23}'s~\cite{DBLP:journals/infsof/Siebert23} observation that Pearl's framework has gained some traction in SE\@.
Structural equation modeling (2 papers), Granger causality (2 papers), and causal impact analysis (1 paper) round out the remaining categories.

\paragraph{Implications.}
Three patterns emerge from this analysis.
First, the SE community's dominant approach to causal questions---making causal claims from correlational evidence, often with regression controls but without formal identification---mirrors the pattern that \citet{haber2022causal} documented in health research and that \citet{grosz2020taboo} described as a ``taboo'' in psychology.
Second, when SE researchers do employ causal methods, they overwhelmingly use either randomized experiments or causal fault localization---methods that are important but insufficient for the field's most pressing observational questions about tool adoption, process effects, and language design.
Third, the design-based methods that powered the credibility revolution---difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, panel fixed effects---remain virtually unexplored in SE, despite the field's abundance of panel data (repositories with temporal structure, developers contributing across projects, tools adopted in staggered fashion) that is well suited to these methods.
This tutorial aims to bridge that gap.

\section{A Primer on Causal Inference for SE Researchers}
\label{sec:primer}

This section develops the technical foundations for causal inference, using the programming language and defect proneness question as a running example.
We examine why naive approaches fail (Sections~\ref{sec:primer-correlation}--\ref{sec:primer-regression}), formalize key concepts (Sections~\ref{sec:primer-po}--\ref{sec:primer-dags}), survey identification methods (Section~\ref{sec:primer-methods}), and discuss the internal--external validity trade-off (Section~\ref{sec:primer-validity}).

\subsection{The Intellectual Landscape of Causal Inference}
\label{sec:bg-traditions}

Understanding how the causal inference toolkit evolved---and the tensions between competing traditions---equips SE researchers to select the approach best suited to their research question and data structure.

\paragraph{Graphical Causal Models.}
Independently, \citet{pearl2009causality} developed the graphical framework, representing causal relationships as DAGs and introducing the \emph{do-calculus} and \emph{back-door criterion} for determining sufficient adjustment sets.
Where the potential outcomes tradition defines \emph{what} to estimate, DAGs provide a visual tool for arguing \emph{why} assumptions hold---making causal assumptions explicit so that critics can challenge specific edges rather than raising vague concerns about ``omitted variables.''
\citet{morgan2015counterfactuals} bridged the two traditions for social science, and \citet{imbens2020potential} clarified their relative strengths, arguing that potential outcomes are more natural for design-based research while DAGs excel at encoding complex causal structures---a synthesis our tutorial brings to SE audiences.

\paragraph{Modern Advances.}
Recent methodological frontiers extend the toolkit in directions relevant to SE\@.
\citet{chernozhukov2018double} introduced double/debiased machine learning for high-dimensional treatment effect estimation, and \citet{athey2016recursive} developed causal forests for heterogeneous effects---both addressing the high-dimensional feature spaces common in mining software repositories.
\citet{callaway2021difference} and \citet{roth2023whats} revealed that standard two-way fixed effects DiD can produce biased estimates under staggered adoption and heterogeneous effects, providing alternative estimators directly relevant to SE panel data studying tool adoption or language switching.
Sensitivity analysis has matured into an essential complement: \citeauthor{rosenbaum2002observational}'s~\cite{rosenbaum2002observational} bounds on hidden bias, \citeauthor{oster2019unobservable}'s~\cite{oster2019unobservable} coefficient stability framework, and \citeauthor{cinelli2020making}'s~\cite{cinelli2020making} robustness value provide tools for assessing vulnerability to unobserved confounding---none yet applied in SE\@.
A new generation of accessible textbooks~\cite{angrist2009mostly, hernan2020causal, cunningham2021causal, huntingtonklein2021effect} has demonstrated that these methods can be taught effectively to non-specialist audiences; \citeauthor{hernan2020causal}'s~\cite{hernan2020causal} \emph{target trial} framework, which asks researchers to describe the hypothetical randomized trial their observational analysis attempts to emulate, is particularly relevant to SE\@.

\subsection{Why Correlation Does Not Imply Causation}
\label{sec:primer-correlation}

Suppose we observe that functional language projects on GitHub have 20\% fewer defects.
Can we conclude that switching to a functional language would reduce defects by 20\%?
Almost certainly not.
Three mechanisms generate spurious correlations.

\paragraph{Confounding.}
A third variable $Z$ that causally affects both treatment $X$ and outcome $Y$ induces an association even absent a direct causal relationship.
Developer expertise is a plausible confounder: Experienced developers may both prefer functional languages and write fewer bugs.
Let $Y$ denote defect density, $X$ denote language choice, and $Z$ denote expertise.
The observed association decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
    E[Y | X\!=\!1] - E[Y | X\!=\!0] = \underbrace{\tau}_{\text{causal effect}} + \underbrace{\big(E[Z | X\!=\!1] - E[Z | X\!=\!0]\big)}_{\text{selection difference}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial Y}{\partial Z}}_{\text{effect of } Z}
\end{equation}
If $\tau = 0$ but functional language adopters have higher expertise and expertise reduces defects, the observed correlation is a pure artifact of selection.

\paragraph{Reverse Causality.}
The association may reflect $Y$ causing $X$: Teams with persistent quality problems may switch to ``rigorous'' languages like Haskell to impose discipline.
Cross-sectional data cannot distinguish $X \to Y$ from $Y \to X$.

\paragraph{Selection Bias.}
Conditioning on a \emph{collider}---a variable caused by both $X$ and $Y$---induces spurious associations.
Analyzing only ``successful'' GitHub projects---e.g., $>$1,000 stars---conditions on a post-treatment variable that depends on both language choice and code quality, generating artifactual correlations that reveal nothing about the causal effect of language choice.


\subsection{Why Multivariate Regression Usually Does Not Suffice}
\label{sec:primer-regression}

Confronted with confounding, researchers often ``control for'' confounders via multivariate regression.
This approach has fundamental limitations.

\paragraph{Omitted Variable Bias.}
Regression can only adjust for \emph{observed} confounders.
Confounders plausibly affecting both language choice and defect rates---developer expertise, team culture, project complexity, code review practices, testing discipline---are largely unmeasurable from repository data.
If the true model includes an unobserved confounder $U$, the estimated coefficient converges to:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\tau} \xrightarrow{p} \tau + \gamma \cdot \frac{\text{Cov}(X, U \mid Z)}{\text{Var}(X \mid Z)}
\end{equation}
Unless $\text{Cov}(X, U \mid Z) = 0$, the estimate remains biased regardless of how many observed controls are included.

\paragraph{Bad Controls.}
Including the \emph{wrong} controls can introduce bias.
\emph{Mediator bias} arises from conditioning on a variable on the causal pathway ($X \to M \to Y$): Controlling for immutable data structure usage when studying functional languages blocks part of the effect.
\emph{Collider bias} arises from conditioning on a common effect of treatment and outcome ($X \to C \leftarrow Y$): Controlling for project popularity, which depends on both language novelty and quality, induces a spurious association.
Without a causal model, researchers cannot determine which controls are appropriate; a regression with 100 covariates is not more causally credible than one with five.

\paragraph{When Can Regression Support Causal Claims?}
Regression identifies causal effects under \emph{conditional ignorability}---selection on observables:
\begin{equation}
    (Y(1), Y(0)) \perp D \mid X
    \label{eq:cond-ignorability}
\end{equation}
This requires that all confounders be measured and correctly included, no post-treatment variables contaminate the model, and sufficient overlap exists between groups.
Propensity score matching~\cite{rosenbaum1983central}, inverse probability weighting, and regression adjustment all rest on this same assumption.
Credibility hinges on encoding the assumed data-generating process as a causal DAG (Section~\ref{sec:primer-dags}) and showing the covariates satisfy the back-door criterion.
Design-based methods---DiD, RDD, IV---appear more credible not because they are assumption-free, but because their assumptions---parallel trends, continuity, exclusion restrictions---are more transparent and partially testable.


\subsection{The Potential Outcomes Framework}
\label{sec:primer-po}

The potential outcomes framework~\cite{neyman1923application, rubin1974estimating} defines causal effects and the assumptions required to estimate them.

\paragraph{Notation and Setup.}
Each unit $i$ has two \emph{potential outcomes}: $Y_i(1)$ under treatment and $Y_i(0)$ under control.
The individual causal effect $\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$ is unobservable because only one potential outcome is realized---the ``fundamental problem of causal inference.''

\paragraph{Causal Estimands.}
We target population-level summaries.
The \emph{Average Treatment Effect} (ATE) is $\text{ATE} = E[Y(1)] - E[Y(0)]$.
The \emph{Average Treatment Effect on the Treated} (ATT) conditions on treated units: $\text{ATT} = E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid D = 1]$.
ATE and ATT coincide only under homogeneous effects or independence of treatment assignment.
Other estimands include the \emph{Local Average Treatment Effect} (LATE) for the subpopulation affected by an instrument, and \emph{conditional average treatment effects} (CATEs).

\paragraph{From Estimands to Identification.}
The naive comparison decomposes as:
\begin{align}
    E[Y \mid D\!=\!1] - E[Y \mid D\!=\!0] &= \underbrace{E[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid D\!=\!1]}_{\text{ATT}} + \underbrace{E[Y(0) \mid D\!=\!1] - E[Y(0) \mid D\!=\!0]}_{\text{Selection Bias}}
    \label{eq:selection-bias}
\end{align}
Selection bias arises because treated and control groups may differ systematically in baseline potential outcomes---e.g., high-ability developers may adopt functional languages precisely because they are high-ability.

In a \emph{randomized controlled trial} (RCT), $(Y(1), Y(0)) \perp D$, so selection bias vanishes and the naive comparison identifies the ATE\@.
A valid RCT additionally requires the \emph{Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption} (SUTVA)---no interference between units and a well-defined treatment.

When randomization is infeasible, the researcher invokes conditional ignorability (Equation~\ref{eq:cond-ignorability}).
Under this assumption plus overlap ($0 < P(D\!=\!1 \mid X\!=\!x) < 1$), the ATE is identified:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ATE} = E_X\!\big[E[Y \mid D\!=\!1, X] - E[Y \mid D\!=\!0, X]\big]
\end{equation}
Credibility hinges on whether all confounders are captured in $X$---a question the graphical framework addresses.


\subsection{Graphical Causal Models and DAGs}
\label{sec:primer-dags}

The potential outcomes framework defines estimands but offers limited guidance on \emph{which} variables to condition on.
\citeauthor{pearl2009causality}'s~\cite{pearl2009causality} graphical framework addresses this by encoding causal assumptions as \emph{directed acyclic graphs} (DAGs).

\paragraph{Directed Acyclic Graphs.}
A DAG consists of nodes (variables) and directed edges ($\to$) representing direct causal effects.
Every arrow is a substantive claim; every absent arrow asserts no direct effect.
This makes assumptions visually explicit---critics can challenge specific edges rather than raising vague concerns about ``omitted variables.''

Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag} presents a plausible DAG for the language--defect question.
Language affects Defect Proneness indirectly through Tooling/Type Safety; Domain, Team Skill, Org Culture, and Codebase Age each influence both Language choice and Defect Proneness, making them confounders.

\paragraph{Causal Paths and Back-Door Paths.}
\emph{Causal paths} follow arrows from treatment to outcome; in Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag}, the sole causal path is Language $\to$ Tooling/Type Safety $\to$ Defect Proneness.
\emph{Back-door paths} connect treatment to outcome through a common cause---e.g., Language $\leftarrow$ Team Skill $\to$ Defect Proneness---transmitting spurious associations.
A naive comparison conflates the genuine causal effect with these spurious associations.

\paragraph{The Back-Door Criterion.}
A set $Z$ satisfies the \emph{back-door criterion}~\cite{pearl2009causality} if: (1)~no variable in $Z$ is a descendant of the treatment, and (2)~$Z$ blocks every back-door path.
When satisfied, the causal effect is identified:
\begin{equation}
    P(Y \mid do(X = x)) = \sum_z P(Y \mid X = x, Z = z)\, P(Z = z)
\end{equation}
In Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag}, $Z = \{\text{Domain},\, \text{Team Skill},\, \text{Org Culture},\, \text{Codebase Age}\}$ satisfies this criterion.
Tooling/Type Safety should \emph{not} be conditioned on---it is a mediator, and conditioning on it blocks part of the effect.

\paragraph{Connecting DAGs to Potential Outcomes.}
When $Z$ satisfies the back-door criterion, the potential outcomes are independent of treatment conditional on $Z$---the graphical counterpart to conditional ignorability.
The two frameworks are complementary: Potential outcomes define \emph{what} to estimate; DAGs argue \emph{why} the required assumptions hold.

\paragraph{Alternative Explanations as Alternative DAGs.}
Alternative explanations correspond to alternative graph structures.
A critic might add developer motivation as an unmeasured confounder, or remove the Language $\to$ Tooling/Type Safety edge entirely.
These disagreements are productive because each alternative DAG implies testable conditional independence relations.

Three points deserve emphasis.
First, different methods estimate different quantities---DiD identifies the ATT, IV recovers the LATE for compliers, RDD identifies an effect at the threshold---so the choice should be guided by the research setting, not convention.
Second, design-based methods derive credibility from features of the research setting rather than covariate completeness, making their assumptions more transparent and partially testable~\cite{angrist2010credibility}.
Third, quasi-experimental methods are not assumption-free; they replace untestable no-unobserved-confounders assumptions with different ones (parallel trends, exclusion restrictions, continuity) whose plausibility must be argued substantively.


\subsection{The Internal--External Validity Trade-Off and the Hierarchy of Evidence}
\label{sec:primer-validity}

Every study navigates a tension between internal validity (causal confidence) and external validity (generalizability).
A controlled experiment assigning developers to different languages achieves strong internal validity but may not reflect production environments; a large-scale GitHub study captures real-world variation but cannot rule out selection bias.

A hierarchy of evidence organizes this trade-off: Randomized experiments provide the strongest causal evidence, followed by quasi-experimental designs---DiD, IV, RDD---with partially testable assumptions, then observational studies with careful controls, and finally descriptive and correlational analyses.
This hierarchy ranks \emph{causal} evidence, not overall scientific value---execution quality matters as much as design choice.
The pragmatic researcher chooses the strongest credible design and builds confidence through \emph{triangulation}: convergent findings across complementary designs, data sources, and populations~\cite{shadish2002experimental}.

\section{The Four-Step Causal Credibility Assessment Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

This section synthesizes the preceding foundations into a practical framework that serves both as a \emph{diagnostic} tool for evaluating existing studies and a \emph{constructive} guide for designing stronger ones.
We illustrate each step using the language--defect question; Sections~\ref{sec:example-a} and~\ref{sec:example-b} demonstrate the framework on published studies.


\subsection{Step 1: Derive an Explicit Causal Theory from Domain Knowledge}
\label{sec:framework-step1}

Before fitting any model, the researcher should articulate the assumed data-generating process so that every causal assumption is visible and debatable: What are the confounders?
Which variables are mediators?
What alternative structures could generate the same associations?

SE papers typically embed these assumptions in prose, making them difficult to evaluate systematically.
A DAG (Section~\ref{sec:primer-dags}) encodes the same assumptions as a formal structure where each arrow is a substantive claim.
For regression-based studies, a DAG is essential because the back-door criterion provides a formal basis for covariate selection, and conditioning on the wrong variables can introduce bias~\cite{wysocki2022statistical}.
For quasi-experimental designs (DiD, RDD), a complete DAG is less critical since identification derives from the design itself, but even a partial DAG clarifies which confounders the design does and does not address.

The plausibility of any causal theory rests on \emph{domain knowledge}~\cite{pearl2009causality}---in SE, this means prior descriptive and qualitative research on the factors shaping adoption, team behavior, and outcomes~\cite{DBLP:conf/icse/Devanbu0B16}.
For the language--defect problem, Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag} encodes the theory, and the back-door criterion identifies the required adjustment set.


\subsection{Step 2: Define the Causal Estimand and Specify Identifying Assumptions}
\label{sec:framework-step2}

The researcher defines the causal estimand in potential outcomes notation and specifies the assumptions under which the estimate is causally interpretable, operationalizing the \emph{estimand-first} principle~\cite{lundberg2021estimand}.
The dominant SE practice of defining research goals as regression coefficients---``the coefficient on language type in Model 3''---prevents readers from evaluating whether the procedures are appropriate.

In the language--defect setting, the estimand might be the ATE of language adoption on defect density.
The back-door criterion applied to Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag} identifies the adjustment set and operationalizes conditional ignorability (Equation~\ref{eq:cond-ignorability}).

This step also determines method choice.
If panel data are available, fixed effects can absorb time-invariant confounders; if the setting features staggered migration events, a DiD design can exploit within-project variation under parallel-trends assumptions that should be probed with pre-trend tests~\cite{roth2023whats}.
Each method's assumptions should be stated explicitly and connected to the causal theory from Step~1.


\subsection{Step 3: Honestly Acknowledge and Mitigate Limitations}
\label{sec:framework-step3}

No study achieves perfect identification, especially in SE where treatments are complex and key constructs---defect proneness, productivity, code quality---lack universally accepted definitions~\cite{DBLP:journals/queue/ForsgrenSMZHB21} and are proxied by noisy repository metrics~\cite{DBLP:journals/ese/KalliamvakouGBS16}.

In the language--defect example, limitations include: unmeasured developer motivation may violate conditional ignorability; the treatment is imprecise in polyglot repositories~\cite{DBLP:conf/wcre/KochharWL16}; and defect proneness is proxied by bug-fix commits, which are noisy and potentially correlated with project-level bug-tracking practices~\cite{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}.

Limitations should be mitigated, not merely listed.
Sensitivity analysis provides principled tools: Rosenbaum bounds~\cite{rosenbaum2002observational} quantify the hidden bias required to alter conclusions; \citeauthor{oster2019unobservable}'s~\cite{oster2019unobservable} coefficient stability framework assesses robustness as controls are added; and \citeauthor{cinelli2020making}'s~\cite{cinelli2020making} robustness value indicates the minimum confounder strength needed to nullify a result.
The goal is to communicate honestly how confident readers should be in the causal interpretation.


\subsection{Step 4: Thoughtfully Navigate Alternative Explanations}
\label{sec:framework-step4}

The researcher should consider rival causal structures that could explain the findings and derive \emph{testable implications} that discriminate among them.
The goal is not to eliminate all alternatives but to engage with the most plausible ones transparently.

In the language--defect example, three challenges illustrate this:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Alternative mechanism.}
    If the benefit of static typing operates through ecosystem maturity rather than the type system itself, conditioning on ecosystem quality proxies should attenuate the language effect.

    \item \emph{Unmeasured confounding.}
    If developer motivation drives both language choice and quality, sensitivity analysis can quantify the required confounding strength.
    \emph{Falsification tests}~\cite{oster2019unobservable} further probe this: If motivation were the driver, one would expect spurious ``effects'' for language features irrelevant to defects---e.g., syntax style.

    \item \emph{Reverse causality.}
    If defect-prone projects migrate to ``safer'' languages, panel data should reveal language switches following defect spikes---a testable temporal signature.
\end{enumerate}

The DAG framework channels disagreements into questions about specific edges and paths rather than vague objections about ``lurking variables''---the kind of structured discourse that could have advanced the subsequent debate~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-07393, DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894} surrounding \citeauthor{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}'s study.

\section{Worked Example A: The PL--Defect Debate}
\label{sec:example-a}

\subsection{The Programming Language vs.\ Defect Proneness Debate}
\label{sec:bg-pl-debate}

The programming language and defect proneness question serves as our running example because it has generated a sustained research thread illustrating the full spectrum of methodological approaches and their identification challenges, progressing through three distinguishable phases.

\paragraph{Controlled Experiments.}
The first phase traded ecological validity for internal validity.
\citet{DBLP:journals/computer/Prechelt00} had 80 programmers implement the same task in seven languages, finding that within-language individual differences typically exceeded between-language differences---a finding that would haunt subsequent observational studies.
\citeauthor{DBLP:conf/oopsla/Hanenberg10}'s~\cite{DBLP:conf/oopsla/Hanenberg10, DBLP:conf/oopsla/MayerHRTS12, DBLP:journals/ese/HanenbergKRTS14} sustained research program then isolated the type system dimension using a custom language with randomized assignment, progressively revealing that static types benefit understanding unfamiliar code and fixing type-related errors but offer no advantage for semantic errors---the defect category most relevant to the PL--quality debate that subsequent observational studies would obscure.
\citet{DBLP:conf/icse/NanzF15} bridged the experimental and observational traditions by exploiting Rosetta Code as a quasi-experimental setting, finding compiled strongly typed languages less prone to runtime failures.

\paragraph{Large-Scale Observational Studies.}
The second phase began when \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14, DBLP:journals/cacm/RayPDF17} shifted to large-scale GitHub data, reporting a ``modest'' correlation between language design and defect proneness---findings widely cited in causal terms~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894} despite the authors' hedged framing, illustrating the downstream consequences of the causal language problem that \citet{hernan2018cword} diagnosed.
The fundamental challenge facing all such studies is endogeneity: \citet{DBLP:conf/oopsla/MeyerovichR13} showed that language adoption is driven by libraries, existing codebases, and experience rather than intrinsic features, meaning language choice is confounded by factors that also affect quality, and cross-sectional regression cannot separate these effects.
\citet{DBLP:conf/icse/GaoBB17} partially circumvented the identification problem by annotating pre-fix JavaScript code with TypeScript types, finding that type checkers detect approximately 15\% of public bugs---a tangible but bounded benefit consistent with the experimental evidence, bridging the gap between controlled experiments and uncontrolled observational studies.

\paragraph{Reproduction and Toward Causal Identification.}
The third phase shifted attention from ``what are the correlations?'' to ``can any correlational approach answer the causal question?''
\citet{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} conducted a meticulous reproduction, reducing statistically significant language associations from 11 to 4 with exceedingly small effect sizes, and \citet{DBLP:journals/tosem/FuriaTF22} reinforced through Bayesian analysis that project-specific characteristics dominate language effects---demonstrating that better statistics alone cannot solve the identification problem.
\citeauthor{DBLP:journals/tosem/FuriaTF24}~\cite{DBLP:journals/tosem/FuriaTF24} took the crucial next step by applying structural causal models to coding competition data, finding ``considerable differences between a purely associational and a causal analysis of the very same data''---the first formal causal identification in the PL--effects literature, though limited to competition rather than real-world project data.
\citet{DBLP:conf/msr/BognerM22} compared JavaScript with TypeScript GitHub projects, finding better code quality but a 60\% higher bug-fix ratio for TypeScript---a result consistent with selection bias, as TypeScript-adopting teams likely differ systematically in code review culture, bug-tracking discipline, and project complexity.
We use this study as Worked Example~B\@.
No existing study of real-world PL effects exploits within-repository panel variation or design-based identification from natural experiments such as TypeScript migrations; our worked examples address this gap.
