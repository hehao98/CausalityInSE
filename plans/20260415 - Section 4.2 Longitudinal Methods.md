## Paragraph-by-Paragraph Plan

### 1. Introductory paragraph (~300 words)
- [ ] Transition from Section 4.1: the cross-sectional analysis revealed that the estimator is not the bottleneck — unobserved confounders are. OLS, PSM, and IPW all converged to similar estimates (66–101%) because none can address time-invariant unobservables like team skill. The problem is identification, not estimation.
- [ ] Key insight (plain language): instead of comparing *different* repos (cross-section), we now compare the *same* repo over time. When we look at the same repository before and after it adopts AI tools, every time-invariant characteristic — team skill, project culture, codebase complexity, programming language — is held constant by construction. We don't need to measure or control for these factors; they cancel out in the within-unit comparison. This is the fundamental advantage of panel data and the core idea behind design-based identification (Section 3.3.3).
- [ ] Describe the panel: 999 repos × 27 months (Jan 2024 – Mar 2026), 240 ever-treated with staggered adoption timing (Figure: `treatment_timing.pdf`).
- [ ] Preview the three-stage progression: (1) naive before/after, (2) interrupted time series with repo fixed effects, (3) modern difference-in-differences. Each stage relaxes one strong assumption by introducing a new design element, at the cost of a different (weaker) assumption.

### 2. Before-and-After Comparison (~250 words)
- [ ] Simplest within-unit estimator: for each treated repo, compare mean monthly commits (log-scale) before vs. after the first AI config commit. This is the longitudinal analogue of the raw group comparison in Section 4.1, but now within-unit.
- [ ] Report paired t-test result: mean change = 0.940 (95% CI [0.710, 1.180]), i.e., treated repos see a 156% (±31%) increase in monthly commits after adoption.
- [ ] **What this buys us:** The within-unit pairing absorbs all time-invariant confounders that plagued the cross-section — team skill, project type, organizational practices. These factors are identical before and after adoption for the same repo, so they cannot drive the observed change. This is exactly the shift from selection-on-observables to design-based identification that Section 3.3.3 advocates.
- [ ] **Identifying assumption (intuitive):** The only way this estimate is causal is if *nothing else* about the treated repos changed between the pre and post periods, besides the treatment itself. In other words, the counterfactual outcome — what commits would have looked like without AI adoption — stayed flat. This is a very strong assumption. If all repos are growing over 2024–2026 (e.g., due to platform growth, seasonal effects, or the general rise of AI-assisted development), then the before/after estimate captures the treatment effect *plus* the secular trend. The 156% figure is almost certainly too high.
- [ ] This motivates modeling the trend explicitly.

### 3. Interrupted Time Series (~400 words)
- [ ] ITS addresses the before/after's key weakness by explicitly modeling the pre-treatment trend. The regression specification is:
  $$Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta \cdot t + \delta \cdot \text{Post}_{it} + \gamma \cdot \text{TimeSince}_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}$$
  where $Y_{it}$ is log(1 + monthly commits) for repo $i$ in month $t$; $\alpha_i$ is a repo fixed effect (one intercept per repo); $\beta \cdot t$ captures the common linear time trend; $\text{Post}_{it}$ is 1 after adoption, 0 before; and $\text{TimeSince}_{it}$ counts months since adoption (0 before adoption), allowing the slope to change post-treatment.
- [ ] **What each term buys us:**
  - $\alpha_i$ absorbs ALL time-invariant unobserved confounders — the exact variables (team skill, project culture, codebase complexity) that made the cross-sectional analysis in Section 4.1 non-credible. This is the same advantage as before/after, but now formalized in a regression framework.
  - $\beta \cdot t$ models a common linear secular trend, so the estimate of $\delta$ is purged of the trend that contaminated the before/after comparison. ITS thus addresses *two* of the cross-section's core problems simultaneously.
  - $\delta$ tests for a discrete level shift at adoption; $\gamma$ tests whether the post-treatment growth rate differs from the pre-treatment trend.
- [ ] Report ITS level-shift estimate: $\hat{\delta} = 0.790$ (95% CI [0.550, 1.020]), i.e., 120% (±26%). The estimate is lower than before/after (156%), confirming that some of the before/after effect was driven by secular trends that ITS now absorbs.
- [ ] Include the ITS box plot figure (Figure: `its_boxplot.pdf`). Caption notes that timelines are artificially aligned to each repo's own adoption date.
- [ ] **Identifying assumption (intuitive):** For $\hat{\delta}$ to be causal, the pre-treatment linear trend must be correctly specified, and no other event must systematically coincide with AI adoption. Unlike before/after, ITS allows for a secular trend — but it assumes the trend is linear and that the *only* thing that changes at the adoption date is the treatment itself.
- [ ] **What remains confounded:** ITS uses only treated repos — there is no control group. This means any shock that happens around the adoption date is indistinguishable from the treatment effect: a team expansion, a major release, a GitHub platform change, or a field-wide shift in development practices. If such events systematically co-occur with AI adoption, $\hat{\delta}$ absorbs them. Additionally, if the true pre-treatment dynamics are nonlinear (e.g., accelerating growth), the linear trend specification may itself be misspecified.
- [ ] **A note on formalization:** Unlike DiD, ITS does not have a standard formalization through the potential outcomes framework (cf. Angrist & Pischke 2009, Cunningham 2021). The assumptions are stated as restrictions on the regression model rather than as properties of potential outcomes. This is one reason why ITS is considered a weaker identification strategy than DiD — the assumptions are harder to state precisely and harder to test.
- [ ] No amount of trend modeling can substitute for the absence of a control group. This motivates the key insight of design-based identification: we need a **control group** to difference out common shocks.

### 4. Difference-in-Differences: Intuition and TWFE (~400 words)

- [ ] **The design-based leap:** Add a control group of 759 repos that never adopted AI tools. The key idea is simple but powerful: any time-varying shock that affects both treated and control repos equally is differenced out, *even without modeling it explicitly*.
- [ ] **Classic 2×2 DiD as intuition.** In the simplest case (one treatment group, one control group, two periods), the DiD estimator is:
  $$\hat{\tau}^{DiD} = (\bar{Y}^{\text{post}}_{\text{treated}} - \bar{Y}^{\text{pre}}_{\text{treated}}) - (\bar{Y}^{\text{post}}_{\text{control}} - \bar{Y}^{\text{pre}}_{\text{control}})$$
  The first difference (within the parentheses) removes unit fixed effects — just like before/after. The second difference (between the parentheses) removes common time shocks — exactly what ITS cannot do. The double difference isolates the treatment effect.
- [ ] **TWFE regression specification (exposition only):**
  $$Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \tau D_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}$$
  This is a compact way to express the same idea. $\alpha_i$ absorbs all time-invariant confounders (like ITS). $\gamma_t$ absorbs **common time shocks** — the new element that the control group provides. $\hat{\tau}$ is the DiD estimate. We present this specification for intuition only and do not report its estimate.
- [ ] **Identifying assumption — parallel trends:**
  $$E[Y_{it}(0) - Y_{is}(0) \mid \text{treated}] = E[Y_{it}(0) - Y_{is}(0) \mid \text{control}] \quad \forall\, t, s$$
  In plain language: absent treatment, treated and control repos would have followed the same *trend* in outcomes. This does not require the two groups to have the same *level* of commits — only the same trend. This is strictly weaker than ITS's assumption: common shocks are fine as long as they affect both groups similarly. It is also weaker than before/after: arbitrary trends are allowed, as long as they are parallel across groups. We also require a **no-anticipation assumption**: repos do not change behavior before they adopt.
- [ ] **TWFE's problem under staggered adoption.** In our setting, repos adopt at different months. Classic TWFE implicitly uses already-treated repos as controls for later-treated repos. When treatment effects vary across cohorts or over time, this produces negative weights on some group-time ATTs, biasing $\hat{\tau}$ (Goodman-Bacon 2021, de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfoeuille 2020; see Roth et al. 2023 for a survey). This motivates modern estimators designed for staggered adoption with heterogeneous effects.

### 5. Two Modern DiD Estimators (~400 words)

#### 5a. Borusyak et al. (2024) Imputation DiD (~200 words)
- [ ] **Idea:** Estimate what outcomes *would have been* without treatment, then compare to what actually happened. The procedure has three steps:
  1. **First-stage (counterfactual model):** Fit a regression on all untreated observations (never-treated repos + pre-treatment periods of treated repos):
     $$Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + X_{it}\beta + \varepsilon_{it} \quad \text{for all } (i,t) \text{ with } D_{it} = 0$$
     This includes time-varying covariates $X_{it}$: log(contributors, stars, issues, forks, releases).
  2. **Imputation:** For each treated observation, plug in the estimated coefficients to predict the counterfactual: $\hat{Y}_{it}(0) = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\gamma}_t + X_{it}\hat{\beta}$.
  3. **ATT as average residual:** $\hat{\tau} = \text{mean}(Y_{it} - \hat{Y}_{it}(0))$ across all treated observations, weighting each treated (repo, month) cell equally.
- [ ] Report $\hat{\tau} = 0.281$ (SE = 0.060), i.e., 32% (±8%).
- [ ] **Key property:** Only untreated observations contribute to the counterfactual model; treated cells are never used as controls for other treated cells, avoiding the negative-weighting problem of TWFE.

#### 5b. Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) DiD (~200 words)
- [ ] **Idea:** Estimate separate treatment effects for each adoption cohort (group of repos that adopt in the same month), then aggregate. For each cohort $g$ and time period $t$:
  $$ATT(g, t) = E[Y_t - Y_{g-1} \mid \text{cohort } g] - E[Y_t - Y_{g-1} \mid \text{never-treated}]$$
  This is just a difference in outcome changes between cohort-$g$ repos and never-treated repos, measured from baseline $g-1$. We use no covariates (`xformla = ~1`), so this reduces to simple differences in means.
- [ ] **Aggregation:** The overall ATT averages the group-time effects, weighting by cohort size. This differs from Borusyak's equal cell-weighting: CS gives each *cohort* equal per-unit weight, while Borusyak gives each *treated cell* equal weight. With heterogeneous effects, different weights produce different aggregates.
- [ ] Report $\hat{\tau} = 0.416$ (SE = 0.090), i.e., 52% (±14%).
- [ ] **Pre-trends tests.** Both estimators support event-study decompositions. Pre-treatment "effects" should be zero under parallel trends. Results: Borusyak joint Wald test, $p = 0.70$; CS simultaneous confidence bands (controlling family-wise error), 0/12 pre-treatment periods significant. Pre-trends tests support, but cannot prove, the parallel trends assumption.

### 6. Why the Estimates Diverge — The Pedagogical Payoff (~350 words)

This is the interpretive heart of the section, connecting back to DAG reasoning from Section 3.

- [ ] **Covariate attenuation in Borusyak (mediator bias).** The critical detail: in the imputation step, the Borusyak model plugs in the **post-treatment values** of the covariates $X_{it}$ to predict the counterfactual $\hat{Y}_{it}(0)$. If AI adoption causes an increase in contributors or stars (because the tool makes the project more productive and visible), and those in turn drive more commits, then the covariates sit on the **causal pathway** $D_{it} \to X_{it} \to Y_{it}$. Conditioning on them absorbs part of the treatment's effect, inflating the imputed counterfactual and shrinking the estimated ATT.
- [ ] This is a concrete, numbers-in-hand instance of the **mediator bias** from Section 3.2: conditioning on a variable on the causal path between treatment and outcome attenuates the total effect. The Borusyak ATT of 32% captures only the effect of AI adoption that does *not* flow through the covariates.
- [ ] The CS specification omits covariates entirely, so it is unaffected by this mechanism. Its ATT of 52% targets the total effect under unconditional parallel trends — but this comes with its own trade-off: if parallel trends holds only *conditional* on covariates, the unconditional estimate may be biased. In practice, the pre-trends tests suggest unconditional parallel trends is reasonable here.
- [ ] **Aggregation weights.** Even without covariate attenuation, the two estimators would differ because they weight heterogeneous cohort-level effects differently. With treatment effect heterogeneity across adoption cohorts, different weighting schemes produce different aggregates — a well-known issue in the recent DiD literature (Roth et al. 2023).
- [ ] **Frame this as a feature.** The divergence is informative, not a failure. The pair of estimates brackets a plausible ATT range of 32–52%. This teaches readers that methodological choices about covariate handling and aggregation have substantive consequences — a concrete demonstration of the DAG reasoning from Section 3.

### 7. Event Study and Pre-Trends (~200 words)
- [ ] Present the combined event study plot (Figure: `event_study_comparison.pdf`). Explain event-study intuition: estimate a separate treatment effect $\tau_k$ for each month $k$ relative to adoption. Pre-treatment coefficients ($k < 0$) should be zero under parallel trends — they serve as a diagnostic. Post-treatment coefficients ($k \geq 0$) trace the dynamic treatment effect over time.
- [ ] Caption: pre-treatment coefficients near zero for both estimators support parallel trends; post-treatment coefficients show CS consistently above Borusyak, consistent with covariate attenuation.
- [ ] Discuss formal tests: Borusyak joint Wald test ($H_0$: all 12 pre-treatment coefficients are jointly zero, $p = 0.70$). CS uses simultaneous confidence bands from the `did` package, which control the family-wise error rate — a more conservative test than pointwise CIs. Result: 0/12 periods significant under simultaneous bands.
- [ ] Caveat: passing a pre-trends test does not *prove* parallel trends (Roth 2022); it only fails to reject a necessary condition.

### 8. Robustness (~200 words)
- [ ] **Trimmed control group:** Drop repos with < 10 total commits (structurally inactive). The concern: if inactive repos have flat outcomes that do not track the counterfactual trend of treated repos, they could violate parallel trends. Borusyak ATT barely changes (0.28 → 0.28), suggesting the result is not driven by inactive controls.
- [ ] **PSM-matched panel:** Restrict the control group to repos matched on pre-treatment observables via propensity score matching. Similar estimate (0.30). Confirms robustness to control group composition. Full analysis in the notebook.
- [ ] Robustness checks probe specific threats to internal validity but cannot prove the identifying assumptions. They are best understood as ruling out specific alternative explanations.

### 9. Longitudinal Takeaway (~400 words)

#### The summary table (assumption progression + estimates)

This table is the capstone of Section 4.2. It appears here — after the reader has seen each method in action — so that assumptions and estimates can be compared side by side. The effect estimate is on the log(1 + commits) scale; percentage change = (exp(Estimate) − 1) × 100%, with ± computed via the delta method (exp(Estimate) × SE × 100%). Note: OLS approximates the ATE; PSM and IPW target the ATT; longitudinal methods all target the ATT. The column is labeled "Estimate" rather than "ATT" because the estimands differ across methods.

| Method | Identifying assumption | What it cannot handle | Estimate | SE | % change |

| **Cross-section: OLS** (§4.1) | Conditional ignorability (all confounders measured) | Time-invariant unobservables (team skill, project culture, codebase complexity) | 0.696 | 0.090 | 101% (±18%) |
| **Cross-section: PSM** (§4.1) | Conditional ignorability (same) | Same as OLS — estimator change alone cannot fix identification | 0.574 | 0.094 | 78% (±17%) |
| **Cross-section: IPW** (§4.1) | Conditional ignorability (same) | Same as OLS | 0.509 | 0.091 | 66% (±15%) |

#### Narrative around the table

- [ ] Core message: the **design** matters more than the estimator. This mirrors the cross-sectional takeaway (Section 4.1) where OLS/PSM/IPW converged on similar numbers (66–101%) — but now the design choices (no control group → ITS → DiD) drive the results dramatically. The cross-sectional estimates (66–101%) collapse to 32–52% once longitudinal designs absorb selection bias.
- [ ] Assumption progression recap: before/after absorbs time-invariant confounders (156%); ITS adds explicit trend modeling (120%); DiD adds a control group for common shocks (32–52%). Each step trades a strong assumption for a weaker one, and the estimates shrink accordingly.
- [ ] The gap between before/after (156%) and DiD (32–52%) quantifies how much of the naive effect was driven by trends and selection, not treatment.
- [ ] Second lesson: within DiD, the Borusyak–CS gap (32% vs. 52%) illustrates mediator bias (Section 3.2) — conditioning on post-treatment covariates attenuates the estimate. This is a concrete, numbers-in-hand demonstration of the DAG reasoning from Section 3.
- [ ] Remaining threats: parallel trends violations (partially testable via event study but never provable), anticipation effects, SUTVA violations, and treatment effect heterogeneity. These threats are **more transparent and partially testable** than the "all confounders measured" assumption of cross-sectional methods — making the identification strategy open to scrutiny, which is the central thesis of this paper (Section 1).
- [ ] Practical takeaway for SE researchers: When longitudinal data is available, design-based identification that exploits within-unit variation is substantially more credible than cross-sectional methods, even with sophisticated estimators. The improvement comes not from statistical machinery but from the **research design**.
