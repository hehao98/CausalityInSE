# Plan: Frequently Asked Questions Appendix

## Design Rationale

The FAQ appendix addresses questions that arise naturally after reading the tutorial but fall outside the main narrative. Rather than organizing by topic, we design the FAQs around **three reader personas** whose backgrounds generate distinct categories of resistance, confusion, and curiosity. Each FAQ should be self-contained (1--3 paragraphs), cite the relevant primer/guide subsection for deeper treatment, and be honest about limitations.

---

## Reader Personas

### Persona A: The Sharp SE Professor

A tenured professor with rigorous CS training and a strong publication record in empirical SE. They routinely run regressions on repository data, report threats to validity, and review MSR papers. They have never taken a course in causal inference or econometrics. Their instinct is that their current methods work fine---they just need better data or more controls. Their questions are skeptical but open: *"Convince me this is worth learning."*

### Persona B: The Econometrics Cynic

A methodologist from economics or statistics. They live and breathe identification strategies, have strong priors that observational evidence is fundamentally fragile, and view anything short of a randomized experiment (or a very clean natural experiment) with deep suspicion. They know nothing about SE research, its data, or its publication norms. Their questions are sharp: *"Why should I believe any of this works in your noisy, messy domain?"*

### Persona C: The New PhD Student

A first-year PhD student directed by their advisor to "do something with causal inference." They have a CS undergraduate degree, can program, but have only taken an introductory statistics course. They may not know what a DAG is. Their questions are practical and foundational: *"Where do I start? What tools do I use? How do I not embarrass myself?"*

## FAQ Inventory

### Category 1: Foundational and Philosophical

These questions address the conceptual underpinnings---what causation means, whether the enterprise is even feasible, and why SE researchers should care.

- [ ] **FAQ 1.1: In complex software systems, a complete causal model might require thousands of variables. Does that mean causal inference is hopeless without a complete theory?** *(Existing placeholder; Personas A, B)*

  **Key points:** Science has never required complete theories. The back-door criterion requires blocking *all* back-door paths, not enumerating all variables---a DAG with 8 well-chosen nodes can achieve identification that a regression with 200 covariates cannot. Design-based methods sidestep the completeness problem entirely by exploiting quasi-random variation. The philosophical stance: partial, transparent causal reasoning is better than implicit, unexamined causal assumptions hiding inside every regression.

- [ ] **FAQ 1.2: My regression has $R^2 = 0.99$. Does that mean I have controlled for all confounders?** *(Existing placeholder; Persona A)*

  **Key points:** No. $R^2$ measures *predictive* fit, not causal identification. A model can have near-perfect $R^2$ while including mediators (blocking the causal path) and colliders (opening spurious paths). Conversely, a correctly specified causal model may have low $R^2$ because many factors affect the outcome but are unrelated to the treatment. The relationship between $R^2$ and confounding is non-monotonic: adding variables can increase $R^2$ while *increasing* bias. Oster's coefficient stability test exploits the *pattern* of $R^2$ changes as controls are added, but $R^2$ alone says nothing about identification.

- [ ] **FAQ 1.3: I have a multivariate regression with many covariates. Doesn't controlling for more variables always reduce bias?** *(Existing placeholder; Personas A, C)*

  **Key points:** No---this is the "Table 2 fallacy" writ large. Adding variables can introduce three distinct problems: mediator bias (blocking the causal path), collider bias (opening spurious paths), and overcontrol bias (conditioning on downstream consequences of treatment). The DAG determines which variables help and which hurt. "Throwing in everything available" is not a strategy---it is a gamble whose expected payoff depends entirely on the causal structure, which regression alone cannot reveal.

- [ ] **FAQ 1.4: What is the role of DAGs in selection-on-observables vs. design-based identification?** *(Existing placeholder; Personas A, B)*

  **Key points:** In selection-on-observables, DAGs are *essential*---the back-door criterion is the only principled basis for deciding which covariates to include and exclude. In design-based identification, DAGs play a *supporting* role: the design itself provides identification, but DAGs clarify which confounders the design absorbs, which remain as threats, and whether design-specific assumptions (e.g., exclusion restriction for IV, no time-varying confounders for panel FE) are plausible. A researcher using DiD does not *need* a complete DAG, but one who draws a partial DAG reasons more clearly about what their design does and does not address.

### Category 2: "My Current Methods Work Fine" (Convincing the SE Professor)

These questions address the skepticism of experienced SE researchers who see no pressing need to change their methodology.

- [ ] **FAQ 2.1: I already control for confounders in my regressions and report threats to validity. What does causal inference add?**  *(Persona A)*

  **Key points:** Two things. First, *discipline*: Causal inference forces you to define your estimand before choosing a method, construct a DAG that makes every assumption explicit, and justify each covariate on causal (not statistical) grounds. This discipline often reveals that the study answers a different question than intended, or that a key variable has been wrongly included. Second, *tools*: Design-based methods (DiD, panel FE) can absorb entire categories of unmeasured confounders that no regression can adjust for---they are strictly more powerful in settings where SE data has temporal or panel structure.

- [ ] **FAQ 2.2: What is wrong with interpreting regression coefficients as causal effects? I am careful about my modeling choices.** *(Persona A)*

  **Key points:** A regression coefficient has a causal interpretation *only* when specific identifying assumptions are satisfied---conditional ignorability, correct functional form, no mediator/collider conditioning. Without explicitly stating and defending these assumptions, the coefficient is a descriptive association whose causal content is unknown. The dominant SE practice of fitting a regression, reporting the coefficient on the treatment variable, and interpreting it in causal language in the discussion is exactly the pattern that the "Table 2 fallacy" describes. Being careful about modeling choices (variable selection, outlier treatment, functional form) is necessary but not sufficient: it addresses statistical concerns while leaving the causal identification question unanswered.

- [ ] **FAQ 2.3: I run controlled experiments with developers. Why do I need observational causal inference methods?** *(Persona A)*

  **Key points:** Controlled experiments are the gold standard for internal validity and nothing in this tutorial diminishes their value. But many of the most important SE questions---Does adopting a language reduce defects in production? Does code review improve long-term maintainability? Does CI/CD adoption increase deployment frequency?---cannot be answered with lab experiments because they involve organizational decisions, long time horizons, and populations that cannot be randomly assigned. Observational causal inference methods are designed precisely for these settings. The target trial framework bridges the two worlds: it asks the researcher to describe the experiment they wish they could run and then evaluates how far their observational design departs from that ideal.

- [ ] **FAQ 2.4: If I run a randomized experiment, am I free from worrying about causal reasoning?** *(Personas A, B)*

  **Key points:** No. Randomization is powerful but not a blanket exemption from causal reasoning. Several common situations break or weaken the experimental guarantee. (1) *Small samples:* Randomization balances confounders *in expectation*, but with small $n$ (common in developer studies with 20--40 participants), realized imbalance can be severe---the "treatment" and "control" groups may differ substantially on key characteristics by chance alone. Checking and reporting covariate balance, and using stratified or blocked randomization, is essential. (2) *Non-compliance:* If participants do not follow their assigned treatment (e.g., developers assigned to use a new tool revert to the old one), the naive comparison of assigned groups estimates the intention-to-treat effect, not the treatment effect itself---recovering the latter requires IV reasoning (LATE via the assignment as instrument). (3) *Non-randomizable attributes:* Many SE-relevant "treatments" cannot be randomly assigned---developer experience, gender, educational background, programming language fluency. These are inherent characteristics, not interventions. Studying their effects requires the same observational identification strategies this tutorial covers; calling a group comparison an "experiment" does not change the identification problem. (4) *SUTVA violations:* If treated and control developers interact (e.g., on the same team, same codebase, same Slack channel), the treatment can spill over, violating the independence assumed by the experimental design. (5) *External validity:* A lab experiment with students on toy tasks may have clean identification but tell us nothing about production software at scale---the target trial framework helps evaluate this gap. The broader lesson: causal reasoning is not a tax imposed on observational studies that experiments get to avoid. It is the discipline of thinking clearly about what your evidence can and cannot show, regardless of the study design.

- [ ] **FAQ 2.5: My paper was accepted at a top SE venue without any of this. Why should I change?** *(Persona A)*

  **Key points:** Publication norms evolve. Psychology's replication crisis led to sweeping methodological reforms that would have seemed unnecessary five years before they became mandatory. SE is earlier on this trajectory. Our analysis shows 29% of empirical SE papers ask causal questions but fewer than 4% use causal methods---a gap that reviewers and editors are increasingly aware of. Early adoption is both a contribution to the field and a competitive advantage: a study that explicitly states its identification strategy, constructs a DAG, and conducts sensitivity analysis is harder to criticize and more likely to produce durable findings than one that relies on regression controls and a standard threats-to-validity section.

### Category 3: "SE Data Is Too Messy" (Convincing the Econometrics Cynic)

These questions address the methodologist who doubts that credible causal inference is possible in the software engineering domain.

- [ ] **FAQ 3.1: Software engineering data is noisy, poorly measured, and lacks the clean institutional variation that economics exploits. How can any quasi-experimental design be credible here?** *(Persona B)*

  **Key points:** SE data has genuine measurement challenges (noisy defect proxies, construct validity issues, SUTVA concerns in interconnected ecosystems), and the tutorial acknowledges these honestly. But SE data also has *structural advantages* that many social science datasets lack: repositories provide fine-grained temporal data at the commit level; developers contribute across projects, creating within-person panel variation; tools and practices are adopted in staggered waves, creating natural DiD settings; and platform policies (e.g., GitHub Actions rollout, npm security mandates) create exogenous shocks. The question is not whether SE data is as clean as a minimum-wage natural experiment but whether the available variation, combined with domain knowledge and sensitivity analysis, supports a credible argument.

- [ ] **FAQ 3.2: Parallel trends, exclusion restrictions, strict exogeneity---these assumptions are hard enough to defend in economics. How can SE researchers, who have less training in identification, defend them credibly?** *(Persona B)*

  **Key points:** This is a legitimate concern, and the tutorial does not claim that design-based identification is easy. The guide (Section 4) exists precisely to walk SE researchers through the reasoning process, and the empirical standard (Appendix) provides verifiable criteria for reviewers. The pedagogical bet is that SE researchers---who are trained to reason about complex systems, dependencies, and graph structures---can learn to construct and defend identification arguments if given the right conceptual framework. The alternative (continuing to make implicit causal claims from regression without identification) is worse, not better.

- [ ] **FAQ 3.3: Aren't you just replacing one untestable assumption (no unmeasured confounders) with another (parallel trends, exclusion restriction)? What have you gained?** *(Persona B)*

  **Key points:** Three things. First, *transparency*: Design-based assumptions are named, specific, and debatable; the no-unmeasured-confounders assumption is a blanket claim about the entire world. Second, *partial testability*: Pre-trend tests for DiD, first-stage F-tests for IV, density tests for RDD, and overidentification tests provide empirical evidence (not proof) bearing on the assumptions. Third, *domain-specificity*: The assumption shifts from "I have measured everything" (which SE researchers almost never can defend from repository data) to "the specific variation I exploit is quasi-random" (which is a more defensible claim in settings with staggered adoption, platform shocks, or within-person variation).

- [ ] **FAQ 3.4: What about external validity? A clever identification strategy that exploits a narrow quasi-experiment tells you about a local effect, not a general one. Isn't that useless for practitioners?** *(Persona B)*

  **Key points:** External validity is a real limitation of design-based methods, and the tutorial discusses the internal--external validity trade-off explicitly. But the alternative is not "general causal knowledge from regression"---it is "no causal knowledge at all, disguised as general knowledge." A credible local estimate (e.g., the effect of TypeScript adoption on defects among projects that migrated) is more useful than a biased general estimate (e.g., the correlation between TypeScript and defects across all GitHub projects). Accumulating multiple credible local estimates across settings and populations---triangulation---is how fields build general causal knowledge. This is how economics built the evidence base on minimum wage effects, returns to education, and health insurance coverage.

### Category 4: Practical Guidance (Helping the New PhD Student)

These questions address the practical "how do I actually do this?" concerns of someone starting from scratch.

- [ ] **FAQ 4.1: I am a new PhD student and my advisor wants me to do causal inference research on SE data. Where do I start?** *(Persona C)*

  **Key points:** Start with the causal question, not the method. Read Section 3 of this tutorial for the conceptual foundations, then follow the guide in Section 4. Concretely: (1) Articulate the target trial---what experiment would you run if you could? (2) Sketch a DAG encoding your beliefs about what causes what. (3) Look at your data through the lens of "what quasi-experimental variation exists?"---panel structure, staggered adoption, threshold-based assignment. (4) Choose the method that matches the variation. For textbooks, start with Cunningham (2021) for an accessible introduction with code examples, then Angrist & Pischke (2009) for deeper treatment.

- [ ] **FAQ 4.2: What software tools and packages should I use?** *(Persona C)*

  **Key points:** For **R**: `fixest` (fast panel FE, DiD, IV), `did` (Callaway & Sant'Anna DiD), `rdrobust` (RDD), `MatchIt` (matching), `sensemakr` (sensitivity analysis / robustness values), `dagitty` (DAG construction and analysis). For **Python**: `linearmodels` (panel FE, IV), `causaldata` (textbook datasets), `dowhy` (causal inference framework). For **Stata**: `reghdfe` (high-dimensional FE), `did_multiplegt` (staggered DiD), `rdrobust`. For **DAG drawing**: DAGitty (web app at dagitty.net), TikZ in LaTeX (as in this paper's figures). The choice of tool matters far less than the choice of identification strategy---use whatever language your project already uses.

- [ ] **FAQ 4.3: How do I construct a DAG? I don't know all the causal relationships in my domain.** *(Persona C)*

  **Key points:** You don't need to know all causal relationships---you need to encode your *assumptions* about the ones relevant to your research question. Start with three nodes: treatment, outcome, and the most obvious confounder. Then ask: "What else affects both treatment and outcome?" Add those as confounders. Ask: "Does the treatment affect the outcome through intermediate variables?" Add those as mediators. Ask: "Are there common effects of treatment and outcome?" Those are colliders---do not condition on them. Read prior literature, talk to domain experts, and iterate. A DAG is a living document, not a one-time exercise. The value is in the process of making assumptions explicit, not in getting the DAG "right" on the first try.

- [ ] **FAQ 4.4: My data is cross-sectional (one snapshot in time). Can I still do causal inference?** *(Persona C)*

  **Key points:** Cross-sectional data severely limits causal identification. You cannot use panel FE (no temporal variation), DiD (no before/after comparison), or most design-based methods. Your options are: (1) selection-on-observables (regression, matching, IPW) with a carefully justified DAG and sensitivity analysis---but you must honestly acknowledge that the no-unmeasured-confounders assumption is strong and likely violated in most SE settings; (2) IV if you can find a plausible instrument; (3) RDD if there is a threshold-based assignment mechanism; (4) *reformulate the question* to exploit available variation (Section 4.2.3)---can you collect panel data instead? Can you identify migration or adoption events that create before/after variation? The guide's most creative contribution is often in transforming an intractable cross-sectional question into a tractable longitudinal one.

- [ ] **FAQ 4.5: How do I convince reviewers that my causal claims are credible? What do I need to report?** *(Persona C)*

  **Key points:** Follow the empirical standard in Appendix B. At minimum: state the causal question explicitly, articulate the target trial, define the estimand, name the identification strategy, state the identifying assumptions, justify covariate selection using a DAG or substantive argument, conduct sensitivity analysis or robustness checks, and discuss at least one alternative explanation with evidence. Use causal language only when your identification strategy supports it; use hedged language ("consistent with a causal interpretation under the assumption of...") when it does not. A well-structured limitations section that *mitigates* threats with quantitative tools (sensitivity analysis, falsification tests) is far more convincing than a laundry list of potential problems.

### Category 5: Scope and Boundaries

These questions address what the tutorial does and does not cover, and how it relates to adjacent topics.

- [ ] **FAQ 5.1: What about causal discovery algorithms that learn causal structure from data? Why does this tutorial focus on theory-driven DAGs instead?** *(Personas A, B)*

  **Key points:** Causal discovery algorithms (PC, GES, FCI) attempt to learn DAG structure from observational data under assumptions like faithfulness and causal sufficiency. They are a valid research direction, but they face practical challenges in SE settings: Hulse et al. (2025) showed that over half of discovered edges change across minor data perturbations, and the required assumptions are often implausible with noisy SE data. This tutorial focuses on theory-driven DAGs because (1) they leverage the researcher's domain knowledge---the comparative advantage of SE researchers---and (2) they are compatible with design-based identification, which does not require learning the full causal structure. The two approaches are complementary: data-driven methods can suggest edges to investigate, while domain knowledge adjudicates which edges are plausible.

- [ ] **FAQ 5.2: What about machine learning methods for causal inference, like causal forests or double/debiased ML?** *(Personas A, C)*

  **Key points:** These methods are important advances that the primer mentions (Section 3) but does not cover in depth. Causal forests estimate *heterogeneous* treatment effects (how the effect varies across subgroups), and double/debiased ML allows flexible functional forms while maintaining valid inference. They are powerful tools but they do *not* solve the identification problem: they still require an identification strategy (selection-on-observables or design-based) and the same identifying assumptions. A causal forest with unmeasured confounders produces biased heterogeneous effects, not unbiased ones. For the modal SE researcher who has not yet mastered the basics of identification, learning DiD and panel FE will yield more immediate returns than learning causal forests.

- [ ] **FAQ 5.3: How does this tutorial relate to A/B testing as practiced in industry?** *(Personas A, C)*

  **Key points:** A/B testing is a randomized controlled experiment---the gold standard for internal validity. Nothing in this tutorial replaces A/B testing when it is feasible. The tutorial addresses the vast majority of SE questions where A/B testing is infeasible: studying the effects of language choice, process adoption, or organizational practices in real-world settings where random assignment is impractical or unethical. The target trial framework bridges the two worlds: it asks "what A/B test would I run if I could?" and then evaluates how far the observational design departs from that ideal. Industry practitioners who run A/B tests can also benefit from the causal reasoning framework when analyzing the observational data that surrounds their experiments (e.g., long-term effects after the experiment ends, spillover effects on untreated teams).

## Implementation Notes

- **Length target:** Each FAQ should be 1--3 paragraphs (150--400 words). The entire appendix should not exceed 5--6 pages.
- **Tone:** Direct, honest, slightly conversational. The FAQ format licenses a more accessible register than the main text.
- **Cross-references:** Each FAQ should reference the relevant primer/guide subsection (e.g., "See Section 3.2 for the formal treatment" or "The guide in Section 4.2.3 addresses this directly").
- **Prioritization:** If space is tight, Category 1 (foundational) and Category 4 (practical) are highest priority---they serve the widest audience. Category 3 (cynic) is important for credibility but could be trimmed. Category 5 (scope) could be cut or folded into the main text.
- **Existing placeholders:** The four existing FAQ placeholders in `main.tex` map to FAQs 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, and 1.4 in this plan. They should be revised and expanded following this plan.
