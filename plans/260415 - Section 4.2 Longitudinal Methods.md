## Overall Arc

Section 4.2 follows the same "progression of increasingly credible designs" as the notebook, but now the DiD portion is structured around the **comparison of two modern estimators** rather than a single estimator with robustness checks. The section has five paragraphs plus a takeaway.

## Paragraph-by-Paragraph Plan

### 1. Introductory paragraph (~150 words)
- [ ] Transition from Section 4.1: cross-sectional analysis showed the estimator is not the bottleneck — unobserved confounders are. Within-repo longitudinal variation absorbs time-invariant confounders.
- [ ] Describe the panel: 999 repos × 27 months, 240 ever-treated with staggered adoption (Figure: treatment timing bar chart).
- [ ] Preview the three-stage progression: naive before/after → ITS → modern DiD.

### 2. Before-and-After Comparison (~100 words)
- [ ] Simplest within-unit estimator: for each treated repo, compare mean log(commits) before vs. after the first AI config commit.
- [ ] Report paired t-test result (ATT ≈ 0.94, 95% CI [0.71, 1.18]).
- [ ] Limitation: no trend adjustment, no control group. A secular increase in commit activity (e.g., from platform growth) would be absorbed into the estimate.

### 3. Interrupted Time Series (~200 words)
- [ ] ITS models the pre-treatment trend explicitly via repo FE regression: `log_commits ~ month_idx + post + time_since | repo_id`.
- [ ] Tests for a level shift (coefficient on `post`) and slope change (coefficient on `time_since`) at adoption.
- [ ] Report ITS level-shift estimate (ATT ≈ 0.79, 95% CI [0.55, 1.02]).
- [ ] Include the ITS box plot figure (Figure: `its_boxplot.pdf`). Note in the caption that timelines are artificially aligned to each repo's own adoption date.
- [ ] Key limitation: still no control group. Cannot distinguish the treatment effect from contemporaneous shocks (GitHub platform changes, seasonal patterns, field-wide trends). This motivates the design-based leap.

### 4. Difference-in-Differences (~500 words, the core)

This is the longest and most important paragraph. It contains four logical blocks:

#### 4a. DiD intuition (~150 words)
- [ ] The key idea: add a control group (759 repos that never adopted AI tools) to separate the treatment effect from common time trends.
- [ ] Classic 2×2 diagram as exposition (treated vs. control × pre vs. post). The double difference subtracts out the common trend — exactly what ITS cannot do.
- [ ] TWFE regression specification as exposition: $Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \tau D_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}$. Explain that $\alpha_i$ absorbs time-invariant confounders, $\gamma_t$ absorbs common shocks. **Do not run this.**
- [ ] Flag immediately: in our staggered setting, classic TWFE is biased (cite Goodman-Bacon 2021, de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfoeuille 2020, Roth et al. 2023 survey). Motivate modern estimators.

#### 4b. Two modern estimators (~200 words)
- [ ] **Borusyak et al. (2024) imputation DiD** with time-varying covariates: Fit $Y(0)$ model on untreated obs, impute counterfactuals, average residuals. Avoids negative weighting. First-stage includes log(contributors, stars, issues, forks, releases).
  - Report ATT ≈ 0.28 (SE = 0.06).
- [ ] **Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) outcome-regression DiD** without covariates: Estimate group-time ATTs as simple differences in means against never-treated controls, aggregate across cohorts.
  - Report ATT ≈ 0.42 (SE = 0.09).
- [ ] Both pass pre-trends tests (Borusyak: joint Wald p = 0.70; CS: 0/12 simultaneous bands excluding zero).

#### 4c. Why the estimates diverge — the pedagogical payoff (~150 words)
- [ ] **Covariate attenuation in Borusyak.** The first-stage model uses contemporaneous (post-treatment) covariate values when imputing Y(0) for treated cells. If covariates are post-treatment mediators (AI adoption → more contributors/stars → more commits), the imputed counterfactual is inflated, shrinking the residual. CS omits covariates entirely and is unaffected.
- [ ] **Aggregation weights.** Borusyak weights each treated (i,t) cell equally; CS averages within cohorts then across cohorts weighted by size. Heterogeneous effects + different weights → different aggregates.
- [ ] Frame this as a feature, not a bug: the divergence is informative. The pair of estimates brackets a plausible range [0.28, 0.42]. This teaches readers that methodological choices about covariate handling have substantive consequences.

#### 4d. Event study figure (~50 words)
- [ ] Present the combined event study plot (Figure: `event_study_comparison.pdf`).
- [ ] Caption: pre-treatment coefficients near zero for both estimators support parallel trends; post-treatment coefficients show CS consistently above Borusyak, consistent with covariate attenuation.

### 5. Robustness (~100 words)
- [ ] Trimmed control group: drop repos with < 10 total commits (structurally inactive). Borusyak ATT barely changes (0.28 → 0.28), suggesting the parallel trends assumption is not driven by inactive controls.
- [ ] Briefly mention that PSM-matched panel (not shown in paper, available in notebook) also gives a similar estimate (0.30), confirming robustness to control group composition.

### 6. Longitudinal Takeaway (~200 words)
- [ ] Summary comparison table of all estimates (before/after 0.94, ITS 0.79, Borusyak 0.28, CS 0.42).
- [ ] Core message: the **design** matters more than the estimator. Before/after and ITS, lacking a control group, produce inflated estimates. DiD, by introducing a control group and exploiting staggered adoption, isolates the treatment effect.
- [ ] Second lesson: when time-varying covariates are post-treatment mediators, conditioning on them attenuates the estimate. The Borusyak–CS comparison makes this visible.
- [ ] Contrast DiD range [0.28, 0.42] with cross-sectional estimates from Section 4.1 (which were higher). The difference quantifies selection bias in the cross-section.
- [ ] Remaining threats: parallel trends violations, anticipation effects. These are more transparent and partially testable (via event study) than the "all confounders measured" assumption of cross-sectional methods.
