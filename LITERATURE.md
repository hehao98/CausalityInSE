# Literature Review

## LR1: Existing Tutorials and Methodological Guides in SE

> Ralph, Paul, et al. "Empirical standards for software engineering research." arXiv preprint arXiv:2010.03525 (2020).

Ralph and a large community of contributors developed 19 empirical standards with checklists for authors and reviewers, covering research methods from controlled experiments to case studies and action research. The standards address validity concerns (conclusion, construct, internal, external) and distinguish essential, desirable, and extraordinary attributes for each method, representing the most comprehensive community-endorsed effort to raise empirical rigor in SE. However, they treat causal inference only implicitly: there is no dedicated standard for causal claims from observational data, no guidance on identification strategies, and no discussion of sensitivity analysis or the hierarchy of evidence for causal questions. This absence sets the stage for the subsequent wave of work specifically targeting causal reasoning in SE.

> Siebert, Julien. "Applications of statistical causal inference in software engineering." Information and Software Technology 159 (2023): 107198.

Recognizing the gap left by general methodology guides like Ralph et al.'s empirical standards, Siebert conducted the first systematic mapping study specifically focused on Pearl's graphical causal inference framework as applied in SE, analyzing 25 papers published between 2010 and 2022. The study finds that adoption of structural causal models in SE is recent and fragmented, with most applications concentrated in software quality analysis and no established community of practice. This mapping is foundational because it quantifies the gap between the availability of causal methods and their actual uptake in SE. However, its scope is limited to Pearl's graphical tradition and excludes design-based methods (difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity) and the potential outcomes framework, leaving a significant portion of the causal inference toolkit uncharted for SE audiences.

> Graf-Vlachy, Lorenz, and Stefan Wagner. "Cleaning up confounding: Accounting for endogeneity using instrumental variables and two-stage models." ACM Transactions on Software Engineering and Methodology 33.8 (2024): 1-31.

Where Siebert's mapping catalogued the state of practice, Graf-Vlachy and Wagner took a prescriptive step by introducing instrumental variables and two-stage models to the SE audience as a means of addressing endogeneity in observational studies. The paper provides a step-by-step tutorial on when and how to apply IV estimation, grounding the exposition in SE-relevant examples where treatment assignment is confounded by unobserved factors. This makes it one of the few SE-venue papers that explicitly teaches a causal identification strategy rather than merely applying one, advancing beyond the surveying approach of Siebert (2023). However, it covers only one method (IV/2SLS) and does not situate the technique within the broader landscape of causal inference traditions or offer a general-purpose framework for assessing causal claims.

> Robillard, Martin P., et al. "Communicating study design trade-offs in software engineering." ACM Transactions on Software Engineering and Methodology 33.5 (2024): 1-24.

Complementing the validity-centered framing of the empirical standards, Robillard and colleagues propose replacing the conventional "threats to validity" discussion with a trade-off framing that documents how the benefits of study design decisions outweigh the costs of alternatives. The paper argues that current limitation discussions---of the kind codified by Ralph et al.---rarely provide sufficient insight into design rationale and that a trade-off perspective promotes fairer assessment of research contributions. This reframing is relevant to our work because causal identification inherently involves trade-offs (e.g., internal vs. external validity, precision vs. bias). The gap is that the paper does not engage with causal identification assumptions, sensitivity analysis, or the specific trade-offs involved in choosing among causal identification strategies such as IV, DiD, or matching.

> Wohlin, Claes, et al. Experimentation in Software Engineering. Springer, 2024 (3rd edition).

Wohlin and colleagues provide the standard reference for conducting controlled experiments in SE, a textbook whose first edition (2000) predates all other entries in this section. The 2024 third edition adds material on A/B testing, replications, open science, and updated statistical analysis tools, keeping the book current with evolving SE practice. It remains the primary methodological resource for experimental SE and is relevant as the baseline against which our tutorial extends. Its persistent gap, even after three editions, is that it focuses predominantly on randomized experiments and provides limited guidance on quasi-experimental designs, natural experiments, and the causal analysis of purely observational data---the very settings where Siebert (2023) documented the fragmented adoption of causal methods.

> Furia, Carlo A., and Richard Torkar. "Mitigating omitted variable bias in empirical software engineering." arXiv preprint arXiv:2501.17026 (2025).

Building on the graphical causal model tradition that Siebert (2023) mapped, Furia and Torkar move from description to practical methodology, illustrating omitted variable bias using two SE examples and presenting methods based on structural causal models to investigate, estimate, and mitigate OVB before and after executing empirical studies. They advance the state of practice by emphasizing that even with large datasets, more data alone cannot correct for OVB if the statistical model is misspecified---a point that directly addresses the "big data fallacy" common in mining software repositories research. However, the paper focuses on a single threat to validity rather than providing a comprehensive framework for causal claim assessment, and it does not cover other identification strategies (IV, DiD, RDD) or the sensitivity analysis tools of Cinelli and Hazlett (2020) or Oster (2019).

> Giamattei, Luca, et al. "Causal reasoning in software quality assurance: A systematic review." Information and Software Technology 178 (2025): 107599.

Expanding the scope of Siebert's (2023) mapping study, Giamattei and colleagues systematically reviewed 86 papers spanning 20 years that apply causal reasoning to software quality assurance, covering both causal inference and causal discovery methods. Where Siebert found 25 papers using Pearl's framework, this broader review reveals that "causal reasoning" in SE is often used loosely to include fault localization heuristics and root-cause analysis techniques that do not employ formal causal identification. They find that fault localization is the dominant application, that Pearl's graphical formulation is the preferred approach when formal methods are used, and that tool support for causal SQA has accelerated since 2021. The gap relative to our work is that this review does not address how to assess causal claims in empirical SE research or how to design observational studies with credible identification strategies---it documents what is done but not what should be done.

> Bhatt, Sahil, et al. "Shaky structures: The wobbly world of causal graphs in software analytics." Empirical Software Engineering 30 (2025): 134.

Taking a critical perspective on the causal graph approaches catalogued by Siebert (2023) and Giamattei et al. (2025), Bhatt and colleagues evaluated four causal graph generators (PC, FCI, GES, LiNGAM) on 23 SE datasets across defect prediction, configuration selection, and project management tasks. They found that over half the edges changed across minor perturbations, concluding that specific conclusions about variable relationships may not generalize because generators can reverse those conclusions with small changes to data or settings. This paper is a cautionary advance: it demonstrates empirically that the data-driven causal discovery approach, which dominates SE applications, is unreliable---implicitly strengthening the case for theory-driven DAG construction and design-based identification. The gap is that it diagnoses instability without offering a constructive methodological framework for when and how causal graphs can be reliably used in SE.

> Siebert, Julien, et al. "Special issue on causal modeling and inference in SE." Information and Software Technology 183 (2025): 107754.

Two years after his mapping study, Siebert co-edited this IST special issue, which surveys five accepted papers spanning Bayesian network construction, causal discovery for software vulnerabilities, causal reasoning in SQA, an industrial CausalOps lifecycle, and agile principle assessment via structural equation modeling. The editorial synthesizes the findings of the intervening period---including Giamattei et al.'s (2025) systematic review---and observes that causal method use in SE remains "sporadic" compared to correlation-based machine learning, and that navigating causal methods is initially challenging for SE researchers because techniques originate from econometrics, social sciences, and AI. The editorial marks the emergence of institutional infrastructure (a dedicated special issue, a workshop series) around causality in SE. Its gap is that it focuses almost exclusively on Pearl's graphical paradigm and does not discuss the design-based credibility revolution or the potential outcomes framework that dominate causal practice in economics and political science.

> Frattini, Julian, et al. "Tutorial debriefing: Applied statistical causal inference in requirements engineering." arXiv preprint arXiv:2511.03875 (2025).

Representing the most recent entry, Frattini, Heyn, Torkar, and Feldt present a tutorial on applying statistical causal inference specifically to requirements engineering, advancing beyond the general mappings of Siebert (2023) and Giamattei et al. (2025) to provide hands-on pedagogical material. The tutorial argues that demonstrating the causal benefit of RE tools, processes, and guidelines requires more than correlational evidence, and walks through the construction of causal DAGs for RE-specific research questions. This is the first SE sub-domain tutorial dedicated to causal inference methodology, making it the closest existing work to what our tutorial aims to do at a broader scale. However, it is scoped to requirements engineering, does not offer a general four-step assessment framework, and does not engage with design-based identification strategies or sensitivity analysis---gaps our tutorial directly addresses.

### Summary of Existing Research

The software engineering community has invested substantially in methodological guidance for empirical research, from Wohlin et al.'s foundational experimentation textbook (first published in 2000, now in its third edition) to the ACM SIGSOFT Empirical Standards developed by Ralph et al. (2020). However, this guidance has overwhelmingly focused on controlled experiments, survey methodology, and case study design, with causal inference from observational data receiving only peripheral attention. The trajectory of recent work reveals a field that is gradually awakening to this gap: Siebert's (2023) mapping study first documented the fragmented adoption of Pearl's graphical causal framework in SE, finding only 25 relevant papers over a decade. Subsequent work has advanced in two directions---prescriptive tutorials on specific methods (Graf-Vlachy and Wagner on instrumental variables; Frattini et al. on causal DAGs for requirements engineering) and broader systematic reviews revealing the loose usage of "causal reasoning" across SE (Giamattei et al.'s review of 86 SQA papers). Practical tools for specific threats have also appeared, notably Furia and Torkar's (2025) treatment of omitted variable bias, while Bhatt et al.'s (2025) stability analysis has raised cautionary flags about data-driven causal discovery. Meanwhile, the broader methodological discourse---exemplified by Robillard et al.'s trade-off framing and the IST special issue editorial's call for a structured community---has advanced validity reasoning without specifically addressing the identification assumptions that underpin causal claims. Critically, no existing SE tutorial or guide provides a unified, accessible introduction that spans the potential outcomes framework, graphical causal models, and design-based identification strategies, nor offers a systematic protocol for assessing causal credibility in published research. Our tutorial aims to fill this gap by drawing on multiple causal inference traditions and providing a four-step framework that SE researchers can apply both to evaluate existing work and to design more rigorous studies.

## LR2: Intellectual Traditions of Causal Inference and Causal Claim Assessment

> Snow, John. On the Mode of Communication of Cholera. 2nd ed. John Churchill, 1855.

Snow's investigation of the 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak is widely regarded as the founding case study of epidemiological causal reasoning. By mapping cholera deaths and tracing water supply to specific pumps, Snow demonstrated that contaminated water---not miasma---was the vehicle of transmission, effectively constructing a proto-natural experiment decades before any formal framework for causal inference existed. This work is relevant as the origin story of empirical causal inquiry: Snow's reasoning anticipated the logic of confounding (households on the same street differed only in water supplier) and the importance of identifying a plausible mechanism, both of which remain central to our four-step framework. The gap, of course, is that Snow operated without formal notation, statistical theory, or an explicit counterfactual framework---tools that would not emerge for another seven decades.

> Neyman, Jerzy. "On the application of probability theory to agricultural experiments. Essay on principles. Section 9." Statistical Science 5.4 (1990): 465-472. [Translated from the Polish original, 1923.]

In a 1923 master's thesis written in Polish and not widely available in English until Dabrowska and Speed's 1990 translation, Neyman introduced the potential outcomes notation that would become the conceptual backbone of modern causal inference. He defined the causal effect of a treatment on a plot of land as the difference between two potential yields---one under treatment, one under control---of which only one could ever be observed, thereby articulating the "fundamental problem of causal inference" six decades before Holland (1986) gave it that name. Neyman's framework is relevant because our tutorial's Step 2 (define the estimand and identifying assumptions) is built directly on the potential outcomes logic he originated. The gap is that Neyman's notation was designed for randomized agricultural experiments; extending it to observational settings where treatment is self-selected required the contributions of Rubin (1974) and the design-based tradition that followed.

> Fisher, Ronald A. The Design of Experiments. Oliver and Boyd, 1935.

Fisher developed the theoretical foundations of randomized controlled experiments, establishing randomization as the principled basis for valid causal inference and introducing the concepts of the null hypothesis, significance testing, and the analysis of variance within experimental designs. Where Neyman (1923) had defined potential outcomes, Fisher provided the practical methodology for ensuring that observed differences could be attributed to treatment rather than confounding---by randomly assigning units to treatment and control conditions, all observed and unobserved confounders are balanced in expectation. This contribution is relevant because the RCT remains the gold standard against which all observational causal methods are measured in our hierarchy of evidence (Section 3.6), and Fisher's logic of randomization-based inference underpins the design-based tradition that the credibility revolution would later champion. The gap is that Fisher's framework was designed for settings where randomization is feasible; in SE, where programming languages, tools, and processes cannot be randomly assigned to developers or projects, the challenge is to approximate the inferential guarantees of randomization using observational data.

> Hill, Austin Bradford. "The environment and disease: Association or causation?" Proceedings of the Royal Society of Medicine 58.5 (1965): 295-300.

Three decades after Fisher formalized randomized experiments, Hill addressed the harder question of how to evaluate causal claims when experimentation is infeasible. He proposed nine viewpoints---strength, consistency, specificity, temporality, biological gradient, plausibility, coherence, experiment, and analogy---for assessing whether an observed epidemiological association reflects a genuine causal relationship, drawing on the landmark case of smoking and lung cancer. He explicitly cautioned against treating these as rigid criteria, noting that "none of my nine viewpoints can bring indisputable evidence for or against the cause-and-effect hypothesis." Hill's framework is relevant because our Step 4 (navigate alternative explanations) draws on the spirit of these viewpoints, adapted to the SE context where analogous considerations (e.g., consistency across projects, plausibility from domain knowledge, temporality of commits) apply. The gap is that Hill's viewpoints predate formal identification strategies and require reinterpretation through the lens of the counterfactual and graphical frameworks that subsequent decades would develop.

> Rubin, Donald B. "Estimating causal effects of treatments in randomized and nonrandomized studies." Journal of Educational Psychology 66.5 (1974): 688-701.

Rubin extended Neyman's (1923) potential outcomes notation from randomized experiments to observational settings, establishing what is now known as the Rubin Causal Model. The key conceptual advance was formalizing the conditions under which observational data can substitute for experimental data: if treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes conditional on observed covariates (conditional ignorability), then causal effects can be identified without randomization. This paper bridges the gap between Fisher's experimental ideal and the observational reality that most empirical research---including virtually all SE research---must navigate. The gap is that conditional ignorability is fundamentally untestable, and Rubin's framework provided the identification conditions but not the practical tools (propensity scores, sensitivity analysis) for implementing and probing them, contributions that would come in subsequent decades.

> Rosenbaum, Paul R., and Donald B. Rubin. "The central role of the propensity score in observational studies for causal effects." Biometrika 70.1 (1983): 41-55.

Building on Rubin's (1974) conditional ignorability assumption, Rosenbaum and Rubin introduced the propensity score---the probability of treatment assignment given observed covariates---as a dimension-reducing device that makes covariate adjustment feasible in high-dimensional settings. They proved that conditioning on the propensity score is sufficient for removing confounding due to observed covariates, enabling matching, stratification, and inverse probability weighting as practical alternatives to multivariate regression. This paper is relevant because propensity score methods are among the most widely used tools for causal inference from observational data across disciplines, and their adoption in SE is growing. The gap is that propensity score methods, like all selection-on-observables strategies, cannot address confounding from unobserved variables---a limitation that motivated the design-based identification strategies (IV, DiD, RDD) championed by the subsequent credibility revolution.

> Card, David, and Alan B. Krueger. "Minimum wages and employment: A case study of the fast-food industry in New Jersey and Pennsylvania." American Economic Review 84.4 (1994): 772-793.

Card and Krueger's study of a New Jersey minimum wage increase---comparing employment changes in New Jersey fast-food restaurants to those in neighboring Pennsylvania---became the landmark exemplar of the difference-in-differences identification strategy and one of the most influential empirical papers in economics. By exploiting a policy change that created treatment (New Jersey) and control (Pennsylvania) groups with plausibly parallel pre-treatment trends, they demonstrated that minimum wage increases did not reduce employment, overturning decades of conventional economic wisdom derived from cross-sectional correlations. This paper is relevant because it illustrates the power of design-based causal inference applied to observational data---the same logic that our tutorial advocates for SE, where policy changes (e.g., adopting a new language, tool, or process) create natural experiments that DiD can exploit. The gap is that DiD methodology has advanced substantially since this seminal application, as Roth et al. (2023) document.

> Angrist, Joshua D., Guido W. Imbens, and Donald B. Rubin. "Identification of causal effects using instrumental variables." Journal of the American Statistical Association 91.434 (1996): 444-455.

Angrist, Imbens, and Rubin formalized the conditions under which instrumental variables identify causal effects, proving the landmark result that IV estimates recover the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE)---the causal effect for the subpopulation of "compliers" whose treatment status is shifted by the instrument. This paper unified the econometric IV tradition (which had existed since Wright, 1928) with the potential outcomes framework, providing precise identification conditions (relevance, exclusion, independence, monotonicity) that transformed IV from an algebraic trick into a principled causal identification strategy. The LATE theorem is relevant because it clarifies both the power and the limitations of IV: the estimated effect applies only to compliers, not the entire population, a subtlety that SE researchers must understand when applying IV methods to questions like tool adoption. The gap is that finding credible instruments in SE settings remains challenging, and the paper does not provide domain-specific guidance for identifying plausible instruments in software engineering contexts.

> Shadish, William R., Thomas D. Cook, and Donald T. Campbell. Experimental and Quasi-experimental Designs for Generalized Causal Inference. Houghton Mifflin, 2002.

Building on decades of work by Campbell and colleagues on validity threats, Shadish, Cook, and Campbell codified the foundational treatment of experimental and quasi-experimental validity, organizing threats to causal inference around four types: statistical conclusion validity, internal validity, construct validity, and external validity. The book covers randomized experiments, regression discontinuity, interrupted time series, and various quasi-experimental designs with and without control groups, providing the vocabulary that subsequent causal inference textbooks would refine. This text is relevant because our four-step framework builds on their validity typology, and many of the quasi-experimental designs they describe are directly applicable to SE research settings (e.g., studying the effect of tool adoption using interrupted time series). The gap is that the book predates the modern econometric advances---the "credibility revolution" that Angrist and Pischke would articulate eight years later---including difference-in-differences with staggered adoption, synthetic control methods, and machine learning-based causal inference.

> Pearl, Judea. Causality: Models, Reasoning, and Inference. 2nd ed. Cambridge University Press, 2009.

Pearl developed the graphical causal model framework that provides the second major formal language for reasoning about causation, representing causal relationships as directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) and introducing the do-calculus for deriving causal quantities from observational distributions. Where the potential outcomes tradition (Neyman-Rubin) defines causation through counterfactual comparison and focuses on identification assumptions, Pearl's framework makes causal assumptions visually explicit through graph structure and provides algorithmic criteria---most notably the back-door criterion---for determining which variables to condition on to identify causal effects. This book is essential because our Step 1 (derive explicit causal theory) directly leverages DAGs as the tool for encoding domain knowledge, and the back-door criterion operationalizes our Step 2 for regression-based studies. The gap is that Pearl's framework has been adopted more slowly by applied researchers than the potential outcomes tradition, and the relationship between the two frameworks---a question Imbens (2020) would later address---remains a source of confusion for researchers encountering causal inference for the first time.

> Angrist, Joshua D., and Jorn-Steffen Pischke. "The credibility revolution in empirical economics: How better research design is taking the con out of econometrics." Journal of Economic Perspectives 24.2 (2010): 3-30.

Angrist and Pischke articulate the "credibility revolution" in empirical economics, arguing that the shift toward design-based studies exploiting instrumental variables (Angrist, Imbens, and Rubin, 1996), regression discontinuity, and difference-in-differences (Card and Krueger, 1994) has transformed the field more profoundly than the sensitivity analysis approach that Leamer had advocated in 1983. Where Shadish, Cook, and Campbell (2002) catalogued designs and validity threats in the abstract, Angrist and Pischke documented the actual shift in practice---the growing dominance of studies that derive causal credibility from research design rather than from statistical modeling assumptions. This paper is foundational to our tutorial because the credibility revolution has not been systematically introduced to SE audiences despite the field's heavy reliance on observational data. The gap we address is translating this design-based ethos to the SE context, where natural experiments and quasi-experimental variation are abundant but rarely exploited.

> Morgan, Stephen L., and Christopher Winship. Counterfactuals and Causal Inference: Methods and Principles for Social Research. 2nd ed. Cambridge University Press, 2015.

In the wake of the credibility revolution, Morgan and Winship bridged the two intellectual traditions that had developed largely in parallel---the Neyman-Rubin potential outcomes framework and Pearl's (2009) graphical causal models---for a social science audience, presenting conditioning techniques (matching, propensity scores from Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983), instrumental variables (Angrist, Imbens, and Rubin, 1996), longitudinal methods, and causal mechanisms within a unified counterfactual framework. Advancing beyond the single-tradition treatments that preceded it, the book emphasizes causal effect heterogeneity throughout and stresses the importance of deep causal explanation via mechanisms rather than mere effect estimation. This textbook is relevant because it provides the integrative framing our tutorial seeks to emulate---presenting the potential outcomes and graphical traditions as complementary rather than competing, a synthesis that neither Shadish et al. (2002) nor Angrist and Pischke (2010) attempted. The gap is its social science focus: the confounding structures, data types, and research design opportunities specific to SE (e.g., version control histories, continuous integration logs, polyglot codebases) require domain-specific translation.

> Chernozhukov, Victor, et al. "Double/debiased machine learning for treatment and structural parameters." The Econometrics Journal 21.1 (2018): C1-C68.

Chernozhukov and colleagues introduce the double/debiased machine learning (DML) framework, which extends the credibility revolution's identification strategies into high-dimensional settings by combining machine learning for nuisance parameter estimation with Neyman-orthogonal moment conditions and cross-fitting. Where Angrist and Pischke's (2010) design-based methods assumed low-dimensional control sets, DML delivers root-N consistent, approximately unbiased, and normally distributed estimators of treatment effects even when ML algorithms (random forests, lasso, deep neural nets, boosted trees) are used for covariate adjustment. This framework is relevant to SE because mining software repositories generates high-dimensional feature spaces where traditional regression may be inadequate, and DML provides a principled way to leverage ML's predictive power while maintaining causal interpretability. The gap is that DML has not yet been applied in SE research, and its adoption will require careful consideration of the overlap and unconfoundedness assumptions in SE data contexts.

> Oster, Emily. "Unobservable selection and coefficient stability: Theory and evidence." Journal of Business & Economic Statistics 37.2 (2019): 187-204.

Oster formalizes the common empirical practice of evaluating coefficient stability---examining whether treatment effect estimates change as control variables are added---by developing a theoretical framework that jointly considers coefficient movements and changes in R-squared to bound the bias from unobserved confounders. She extends the earlier Altonji, Elder, and Taber approach and provides practical tools (including the Stata package `psacalc`) for computing bounds on causal effects under proportional selection assumptions, offering researchers a way to assess robustness that complements the design-based strategies of Angrist and Pischke (2010). This paper is relevant because it provides a sensitivity analysis approach well-suited to SE regression studies where researchers routinely present specifications with varying control sets. The gap is that Oster's method has not yet been applied in SE research, and integrating it requires domain-specific guidance on which observed covariates serve as reasonable benchmarks for unobserved confounding.

> Cinelli, Carlos, and Chad Hazlett. "Making sense of sensitivity: Extending omitted variable bias." Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series B 82.1 (2020): 39-67.

Published contemporaneously with Oster's (2019) work on coefficient stability, Cinelli and Hazlett take a complementary approach to sensitivity analysis by extending the omitted variable bias framework to develop tools that do not require assumptions about the functional form of treatment assignment or the distribution of unobserved confounders. They introduce two key metrics---the robustness value (the minimum confounder strength needed to overturn a result) and sensitivity contour plots based on partial R-squared---which improve upon Oster's approach by providing sharper bounds under weaker assumptions. These tools are directly applicable to SE studies that rely on multiple regression for causal claims, as they provide a principled way to assess how robust findings are to unobserved confounding. The gap relative to our work is that these methods need to be illustrated with SE-specific confounding structures and integrated into a broader causal assessment protocol rather than being used in isolation.

> Imbens, Guido W. "Potential outcome and directed acyclic graph approaches to causality: Relevance for empirical practice in economics." Journal of Economic Literature 58.4 (2020): 1129-1179.

A decade after the credibility revolution paper, Imbens stepped back to compare the two dominant causal inference frameworks---potential outcomes (PO) and directed acyclic graphs (DAGs)---arguing that the PO framework is more natural for the design-based research that the credibility revolution had elevated. He identifies five features of the PO framework that explain its dominance in economics, including its ability to capture shape restrictions (e.g., monotonicity for IV) and its focus on well-defined interventions, while acknowledging that Pearl's DAG approach (which Morgan and Winship had integrated) offers strengths for encoding complex causal structures. This essay is essential for our tutorial because it maps the intellectual terrain between the two major traditions that we must reconcile for SE audiences, who currently encounter these frameworks in isolation. The gap is that Imbens writes for economists with extensive causal methods exposure; SE researchers need a more introductory treatment that also illustrates how DAGs and PO complement each other in SE-specific settings.

> Huntington-Klein, Nick. The Effect: An Introduction to Research Design and Causality. Chapman and Hall/CRC, 2021.

Drawing on the pedagogical insights of Morgan and Winship (2015) and the design-based emphasis of Angrist and Pischke (2010), Huntington-Klein provides an accessible introduction to causal inference that prioritizes intuition and graphical reasoning over mathematical formalism, covering identification, regression, matching, difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, and regression discontinuity with code examples in R, Stata, and Python. The book advances causal inference pedagogy by structuring exposition around the question "does X cause Y?" and emphasizing that answering causal questions requires understanding the data-generating process rather than applying statistical techniques mechanically---a framing that lowers the barrier to entry compared to earlier textbooks. This text is relevant as a pedagogical model: it demonstrates that causal inference can be taught effectively to audiences without prior exposure to econometric theory. The gap is that it is not tailored to SE, where the relevant treatments (language choice, tool adoption, process changes), outcomes (defects, productivity, quality), and confounders (developer experience, project complexity, team size) require domain-specific examples and DAGs.

> Roth, Jonathan, et al. "What's trending in difference-in-differences? A synthesis of the recent econometrics literature." Journal of Econometrics 235.2 (2023): 2218-2244.

Roth, Sant'Anna, Bilinski, and Poe synthesize the rapid advances in difference-in-differences methodology that accumulated after Angrist and Pischke's (2010) credibility revolution paper, beginning from a canonical two-period, two-group model and classifying recent contributions as relaxing assumptions about treatment timing (staggered adoption), parallel trends (pre-trend testing, conditional parallel trends), or inference (cluster-robust standard errors). They demonstrate that standard two-way fixed effects regression with heterogeneous treatment effects---the workhorse estimator that many applied researchers inherited from the credibility revolution---can produce misleading results through "forbidden comparisons" that assign negative weights to some treatment effects. This review is relevant because DiD is the identification strategy most naturally suited to SE panel data (e.g., studying tool adoption, language switching, or policy changes over time), yet SE researchers have had no accessible introduction to its modern form. The gap we address is translating these methods to SE settings and illustrating their application with the PL vs. defect debate.

### Summary of Existing Research

The intellectual landscape of causal inference has developed over more than a century through a series of foundational contributions, each building on and responding to its predecessors. The quest for rigorous causal reasoning began with Snow's (1855) pioneering epidemiological investigation of cholera, which demonstrated the power of systematic empirical reasoning about cause and effect long before any formal statistical framework existed. The conceptual foundations were laid in the early twentieth century when Neyman (1923) introduced the potential outcomes notation for agricultural experiments and Fisher (1935) developed the theory of randomized controlled experiments, together establishing the twin pillars of experimental causal inference. Hill (1965) then confronted the harder problem of evaluating causal claims when experimentation is infeasible, proposing nine viewpoints for assessing observational evidence that shaped causal reasoning across all empirical sciences. Rubin (1974) extended the potential outcomes framework to observational settings, formalizing conditional ignorability as the key identification condition, and Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983) introduced the propensity score as a practical tool for implementing covariate adjustment in high-dimensional settings. The design-based tradition matured through landmark applications---Card and Krueger's (1994) difference-in-differences study of minimum wages demonstrated the power of exploiting natural experiments, and Angrist, Imbens, and Rubin (1996) formalized instrumental variable identification through the LATE theorem---while Shadish, Cook, and Campbell (2002) systematized experimental and quasi-experimental validity into a comprehensive typology. Pearl (2009) developed the graphical causal model framework as a complementary formal language, providing algorithmic tools (the back-door criterion, do-calculus) for determining when and how causal effects can be identified from observational data. The "credibility revolution" articulated by Angrist and Pischke (2010) then crystallized the shift toward design-based identification strategies as the primary basis for causal credibility, while Morgan and Winship (2015) bridged the potential outcomes and graphical traditions into a unified counterfactual framework. Imbens (2020) clarified the relative strengths of each tradition from an economist's perspective. Methodological frontiers have continued to advance: Chernozhukov et al. (2018) extended design-based identification into high-dimensional settings via double/debiased machine learning, and Roth et al. (2023) synthesized rapid developments in difference-in-differences methodology that revealed problems with the standard two-way fixed effects estimator. Alongside identification, sensitivity analysis has matured into an essential complement: Oster's (2019) coefficient stability framework and Cinelli and Hazlett's (2020) robustness value approach provide formal tools for assessing how vulnerable causal conclusions are to unobserved confounding. Pedagogically, Huntington-Klein (2021) and Rohrer (2024) have demonstrated that these ideas can be communicated effectively to non-specialist audiences. Despite this rich and evolving methodological toolkit---spanning from Snow's empirical reasoning through a century of formalization to modern sensitivity analysis---no existing work has synthesized these traditions into a unified, accessible framework tailored to the needs and data structures of software engineering research. Our tutorial addresses this gap by drawing on all of these intellectual currents and translating them into a four-step causal credibility assessment protocol with SE-specific examples and guidance.

## LR3: The Programming Language vs. Defect Proneness Debate

> Prechelt, Lutz. "An empirical comparison of seven programming languages." Computer 33.10 (2000): 23-29.

Prechelt conducted one of the earliest controlled comparisons of programming languages, having 80 programmers implement the same "Phonecode" task in C, C++, Java, Perl, Python, Rexx, and Tcl, and measuring program length, programming effort, runtime efficiency, memory consumption, and reliability. The key finding was that scripting languages were more productive than conventional languages for this task, but---critically---differences between individual programmers within the same language were typically larger than differences between languages. This study set an important early benchmark by demonstrating that within-programmer variation dwarfs between-language variation, a finding that would haunt all subsequent observational studies. The gap is its small scale, single task focus, and the fact that programmer self-selection into languages makes it a quasi-experiment rather than a randomized trial, leaving open the question of whether the finding generalizes to real-world software projects.

> Hanenberg, Stefan. "An experiment about static and dynamic type systems: doubts about the positive impact of static type systems on development time." Proceedings of the 25th ACM SIGPLAN Conference on Object-Oriented Programming, Systems, Languages, and Applications (OOPSLA). ACM, 2010, pp. 22-35.

To move beyond Prechelt's (2000) whole-language comparisons and isolate the specific effect of type systems, Hanenberg designed a custom programming language ("Purity") in both a statically typed and a dynamically typed version---identical in every other respect---and randomly assigned 49 subjects to develop a scanner and parser over 27 hours. The randomized design eliminated the self-selection confounding that plagued Prechelt's study, and the result was striking: static type systems showed no statistically significant positive impact on development time, with dynamic typing subjects completing the scanner phase faster (p=0.04). This paper launched a sustained research program of controlled experiments on type systems (Mayer et al., 2012; Hanenberg et al., 2014) and is relevant because it demonstrated that the widely held belief in the productivity benefits of static typing lacks experimental support, at least under controlled conditions. The gap is the artificial language and task, which limits ecological validity, and the focus on development time rather than long-term code quality or defect rates.

> Mayer, Clemens, et al. "An empirical study of the influence of static type systems on the usability of undocumented software." Proceedings of the 27th ACM SIGPLAN Conference on Object-Oriented Programming, Systems, Languages, and Applications (OOPSLA). ACM, 2012, pp. 683-702.

Extending Hanenberg's (2010) randomized experiment from development tasks to maintenance tasks, Mayer, Hanenberg, Robbes, Tanter, and Stefik used 27 subjects performing programming tasks on an undocumented API, again comparing statically and dynamically typed versions of otherwise identical code. Where Hanenberg (2010) found no benefit for static types during initial development, Mayer et al. found mixed results during maintenance: for some tasks static typing led to faster completion, while for others dynamic typing was faster, suggesting that the type system's effect depends on the nature of the task. This paper is relevant because it represents the controlled experimental tradition operating at a finer granularity, addressing the type system dimension that later large-scale observational studies (Ray et al., 2014) would examine with far less internal validity. The persistent gaps are the small sample size, the use of a single artificial API, and the lab setting, which limits ecological validity.

> Meyerovich, Leo A., and Ariel S. Rabkin. "Empirical analysis of programming language adoption." ACM SIGPLAN Notices 48.10 (2013): 1-18.

Complementing the controlled experiments of Prechelt (2000) and Mayer et al. (2012) with an observational lens, Meyerovich and Rabkin studied programming language adoption at scale through surveys of up to 13,000 programmers and mining of over 200,000 SourceForge projects. They found that intrinsic language features play only a secondary role in adoption decisions---developers prioritize available libraries, existing codebases, and their own experience---and that developers perceive static types as more valuable for expressivity than for correctness checking. This paper is relevant because it challenges the implicit assumption underlying all PL-quality studies: that language choice is driven by technical quality concerns. The gap is that the study is descriptive and does not examine quality outcomes, but it provides essential context for understanding the selection mechanisms that would plague the observational studies about to follow.

> Hanenberg, Stefan, Sebastian Kleinschmager, Romain Robbes, Eric Tanter, and Andreas Stefik. "An empirical study on the impact of static typing on software maintainability." Empirical Software Engineering 19.5 (2014): 1335-1382.

In the most comprehensive experiment of the Hanenberg research program, Hanenberg, Kleinschmager, Robbes, Tanter, and Stefik tested whether static type systems improve software maintainability by having subjects perform three distinct task types: understanding undocumented code, fixing type errors, and fixing semantic errors, using Java (statically typed) and Groovy (dynamically typed). The results were nuanced: static types provided significant benefits for understanding unfamiliar code and for fixing type-related errors, but offered no advantage for fixing semantic errors---a critical distinction, since semantic errors (logic bugs) are precisely the kind of defect that the PL-quality debate is most concerned with. This paper is relevant because it decomposes the coarse "does typing affect quality?" question into finer-grained sub-questions and shows that the answer depends on what kind of quality is being measured. The gap is that the experiment still uses a lab setting with relatively small tasks, leaving open whether the findings scale to real-world software maintenance.

> Ray, Baishakhi, et al. "A large scale study of programming languages and code quality in GitHub." Proceedings of the 22nd ACM SIGSOFT International Symposium on Foundations of Software Engineering. 2014.

Shifting from the controlled experiments of the preceding decade to large-scale observational data, Ray, Posnett, Filkov, and Devanbu conducted the landmark study analyzing 729 GitHub projects across 17 programming languages, using negative binomial regression to examine the relationship between language features and defect-fixing commits. They reported that language design has a "modest" effect on software quality---strong typing is somewhat better than weak typing, and functional languages slightly outperform procedural ones---while acknowledging that process factors (project size, team size, commit size) overwhelmingly dominate. This paper is the origin of the PL vs. defect proneness debate and is relevant as the primary worked example in our tutorial. The central gap, which Meyerovich and Rabkin's (2013) adoption study foreshadowed, is that the regression-based approach cannot credibly support causal claims because language choice is confounded by developer experience, project characteristics, and self-selection---issues that the paper's controls do not resolve.

> Nanz, Sebastian, and Carlo A. Furia. "A comparative study of programming languages in Rosetta Code." 37th IEEE/ACM International Conference on Software Engineering (ICSE). IEEE, 2015, pp. 778-788.

Taking a different approach from both the small-scale controlled experiments (Hanenberg, 2010; Mayer et al., 2012) and the large-scale observational studies (Ray et al., 2014), Nanz and Furia exploited the Rosetta Code repository---where multiple programmers independently implement the same algorithmic task in different languages---as a natural quasi-experimental setting for comparing eight languages across conciseness, performance, and failure proneness. By holding the task constant and comparing solutions across languages, this design partially controls for task complexity, a confounder that plagues observational studies of real-world projects. They found that compiled strongly typed languages are less prone to runtime failures than interpreted or weakly typed languages, providing complementary evidence to Hanenberg et al.'s (2014) finding that static types help with type errors. The gap is that Rosetta Code solutions are small, algorithmic exercises written by self-selected contributors, so the findings may not generalize to large-scale software systems---but the study demonstrates a creative middle ground between controlled experiments and uncontrolled observational studies.

> Devanbu, Premkumar, Thomas Zimmermann, and Christian Bird. "Belief & evidence in empirical software engineering." Proceedings of the 38th International Conference on Software Engineering. ACM, 2016.

Shortly after the Ray et al. (2014) study brought PL-quality claims into prominence, Devanbu, Zimmermann, and Bird surveyed 564 Microsoft programmers about their beliefs regarding empirically falsifiable claims about software development, then compared these beliefs against evidence from the programmers' own projects. They found that programmer beliefs are primarily based on personal experience, do not necessarily correspond with reality, and that most widely-held beliefs showed only sporadic empirical support across different projects. This paper provides the motivational backdrop for our tutorial: SE practice is guided by folklore and contested conventional wisdom (including beliefs about PL quality), and resolving contested claims requires methodological tools that go beyond aggregating opinions or running correlational analyses. The gap is that the paper diagnoses the belief-evidence disconnect without offering causal methodology as a path toward resolution.

> Kochhar, Pavneet Singh, Dinusha Wijedasa, and David Lo. "A large scale study of multiple programming languages and code quality." 2016 IEEE 23rd International Conference on Software Analysis, Evolution, and Reengineering (SANER). Vol. 1. IEEE, 2016.

Extending the Ray et al. (2014) study along a new dimension, Kochhar, Wijedasa, and Lo examined how the use of multiple programming languages within a project affects code quality, analyzing GitHub projects with attention to the language mix rather than treating each project as a single-language entity. They found that multilingual projects exhibit different defect patterns than single-language projects, suggesting that language interaction effects are relevant---a dimension that Ray et al.'s single-language-per-project analysis had missed. This paper is relevant because it highlights that real-world projects are polyglot and that treating language as a simple project-level variable obscures within-project variation, foreshadowing the need for finer-grained commit-level analysis. The gap is that the study inherits the same identification challenges as Ray et al.: the association between language mix and defect patterns is confounded by project complexity and architectural decisions.

> Gao, Zheng, Christian Bird, and Earl T. Barr. "To type or not to type: quantifying detectable bugs in JavaScript." Proceedings of the 39th International Conference on Software Engineering (ICSE). IEEE/ACM, 2017, pp. 758-769.

Rather than conducting a controlled experiment with subjects, Gao, Bird, and Barr devised an ingenious quasi-experimental methodology: they identified publicly fixed bugs in JavaScript project histories on GitHub, checked out the pre-fix code, manually added TypeScript or Flow type annotations, and tested whether the type checker would have flagged the buggy code. They found that both Flow and TypeScript successfully detected approximately 15% of public bugs---an under-approximation, since these bugs had already survived testing and code review. This study directly bridges the gap between controlled type-system experiments (Hanenberg, 2010; Hanenberg et al., 2014) and real-world observational studies (Ray et al., 2014): it measures the actual bug-prevention benefit of type systems on real project bugs without relying on regression-based association. The gap is that the study measures only bugs that type checkers could theoretically catch (type errors) and says nothing about the broader category of semantic or logic errors---consistent with Hanenberg et al.'s (2014) finding that static types do not help with semantic bugs.

> Ray, Baishakhi, et al. "A large-scale study of programming languages and code quality in GitHub." Communications of the ACM 60.10 (2017): 91-100.

This extended CACM version of the 2014 FSE paper brought the programming language and code quality findings to a much broader audience, including practitioners and researchers outside the SE community. The wider dissemination amplified both the paper's influence and the downstream causal misinterpretation of its correlational findings, as numerous citing papers and popular press articles reported the results as evidence that certain languages "cause" fewer bugs---an escalation from the original paper's more hedged language. This version is relevant to our tutorial's citation analysis (Section 5.2) because it documents how correlational findings migrate from hedged academic claims to stronger causal interpretations in downstream work. The methodological gap remains unchanged from the original, but the CACM version makes the social consequences of that gap---widespread causal misinterpretation---vividly apparent.

> Berger, Emery D., et al. "On the impact of programming languages on code quality: A reproduction study." ACM Transactions on Programming Languages and Systems (TOPLAS) 41.4 (2019): 1-24.

Berger, Hollenbeck, Maj, Vitek, and Vitek conducted the first rigorous reproduction of Ray et al. (2014), identifying problems with missing code, language classification, and statistical modeling in the original analysis that the CACM (2017) version had not addressed. Their reanalysis reduced the number of languages with statistically significant associations with defects from 11 to only 4, with exceedingly small effect sizes, leading them to conclude that "causation is not supported by the data" and that "too many unaccounted sources of bias remain to hope for a meaningful comparison of bug rates across languages." This reproduction study marks a turning point in the debate: it demonstrates that the original correlational findings are fragile to reasonable methodological variations, vindicating the caution urged by Prechelt's (2000) observation that individual differences dominate language differences. The gap it identifies---that observational regression cannot resolve the confounding problem regardless of how the analysis is specified---is precisely the motivation for the panel fixed effects approach we propose in Section 6.

> Furia, Carlo A., Richard Torkar, and Robert Feldt. "Applying Bayesian analysis guidelines to empirical software engineering data: The case of programming languages and code quality." ACM Transactions on Software Engineering and Methodology 31.1 (2022): 1-38.

After Berger et al. (2019) exposed the fragility of the original frequentist analysis, Furia, Torkar, and Feldt advanced the statistical methodology by reanalyzing the Ray et al. dataset using principled Bayesian methods, demonstrating how to build flexible hierarchical models that properly account for uncertainty and project-level heterogeneity. Their goals were orthogonal to the causal debate---they aimed to showcase Bayesian analysis guidelines rather than resolve the PL-quality question---but they found that project-specific characteristics matter more than programming language choice and that credible intervals for language effects are wide. This paper represents a methodological improvement (Bayesian over frequentist analysis) that reinforces Berger et al.'s conclusion through an entirely different statistical lens. The gap is that Bayesian analysis, however principled, remains correlational without explicit causal identification assumptions---better statistics alone cannot solve the identification problem that lies at the heart of the debate.

> Furia, Carlo A., Richard Torkar, and Robert Feldt. "Towards causal analysis of empirical software engineering data: The impact of programming languages on coding competitions." ACM Transactions on Software Engineering and Methodology 33.1 (2024): 1-35.

Building on their Bayesian reanalysis (2022) and recognizing that improved statistics without identification is insufficient, Furia, Torkar, and Feldt took the crucial next step by applying structural causal models (directed acyclic graphs of causal Bayesian networks) to public data from Google's Code Jam coding competition. They find "considerable differences between a purely associational and a causal analysis of the very same data," demonstrating that even imperfect causal analysis can yield more precise and robust answers than purely predictive techniques---the first paper in the PL-effects literature to explicitly employ formal causal identification. This paper is the most direct predecessor to our work, advancing the debate from "are the correlations robust?" (Berger et al., 2019; Furia et al., 2022) to "what do the causal effects actually look like?" The remaining gaps are that it uses coding competition data rather than real-world software projects, the structural causal model relies on a relatively simple DAG, and it does not exploit panel variation (within-developer or within-repository) that would strengthen identification against the confounders that Meyerovich and Rabkin (2013) documented.

### Summary of Existing Research

The question of whether programming language choice affects software defect proneness has generated a sustained and contentious research thread, advancing through three distinguishable phases. The first phase consists of controlled experiments that traded ecological validity for internal validity. Prechelt (2000) established that within-programmer variation dwarfs between-language variation; Hanenberg (2010) introduced randomized type-system experiments with a custom language, finding no development-time benefit for static typing; Mayer et al. (2012) extended this to maintenance tasks with mixed results; and Hanenberg et al.'s (2014) most comprehensive experiment revealed that static types help with type errors but not semantic errors---a nuance that subsequent observational studies would obscure. Nanz and Furia (2015) bridged the experimental and observational traditions by using Rosetta Code as a natural quasi-experiment, finding compiled strongly typed languages less prone to runtime failures. The second phase began with Ray et al.'s (2014) large-scale observational study of GitHub projects, which traded internal validity for scale and reported modest correlations between language features and defect-related commits; its CACM (2017) version amplified these findings to a broad audience, where they were widely misinterpreted as causal. Gao, Bird, and Barr (2017) offered a more targeted measurement, showing that TypeScript and Flow would have caught approximately 15% of real JavaScript bugs---confirming a tangible but bounded benefit of type systems that was consistent with the experimental evidence. Berger et al.'s (2019) meticulous reproduction then exposed data quality and modeling issues that rendered most reported associations non-significant, marking a turning point that shifted attention from "what are the correlations?" to "can any correlational approach answer the causal question?" The third and current phase has progressively moved the debate toward more principled methodological footing: Furia, Torkar, and Feldt first applied Bayesian analysis guidelines (2022) and then structural causal models (2024), demonstrating that associational and causal analyses of the same data diverge substantially. Surrounding these core papers, Meyerovich and Rabkin's (2013) work on adoption and Devanbu et al.'s (2016) study of developer beliefs have documented the social and cognitive factors that make language choice endogenous, while Kochhar et al. (2016) highlighted the polyglot reality of modern software projects. Despite this progression---from controlled experiments through observational studies to causal modeling---a fundamental identification gap persists: no existing study of real-world PL effects exploits within-developer or within-repository variation via panel methods to separate language effects from the confounders that determine both language choice and defect rates. Our tutorial addresses this gap both diagnostically---applying the four-step causal credibility assessment to show precisely why the existing evidence is causally ambiguous---and constructively, demonstrating how panel fixed effects methods can strengthen identification beyond what any prior study in this literature has achieved.

## LR4: Revisiting Similar Problems in Psychology and Health Research

> Hernán, Miguel A. "The C-word: Scientific euphemisms do not improve causal inference from observational data." American Journal of Public Health 108.5 (2018): 616-619.

In this widely cited editorial, Hernán issued a provocative challenge to the prevailing norm in epidemiology and health research of replacing causal language with associational euphemisms---terms like "association," "link," or "relationship"---when reporting observational findings. He argued that this linguistic evasion does not prevent readers from drawing causal inferences; it merely obscures the causal question, invites analytical errors, and prevents transparent discussion of the identification assumptions required for valid causal claims. This paper is foundational for LR4 because it articulates the core problem we observe in SE: researchers routinely investigate causal questions (does X improve quality? does Y reduce defects?) but report findings using hedged associational language that neither clarifies the estimand nor justifies the identification strategy. The gap relative to our work is that Hernán's argument is a call to action rather than a systematic assessment of the field---it does not quantify the prevalence of causal euphemisms or provide a structured framework for evaluating causal claims, tasks that subsequent papers would take up.

> Rohrer, Julia M. "Thinking clearly about correlations and causation: Graphical causal models for observational data." Advances in Methods and Practices in Psychological Science 1.1 (2018): 27-42.

Published the same year as Hernán's editorial, Rohrer introduced graphical causal models to psychology as a practical tool for reasoning about confounding, mediation, and collider bias in observational data. She demonstrated that common statistical control practices---adding covariates to regression models without explicit causal reasoning---can introduce bias rather than reduce it, a problem that Wysocki, Lawson, and Rhemtulla (2022) would later formalize. This paper advanced the field by providing psychologists with their first accessible primer on DAGs, moving beyond the abstract admonition that "correlation does not imply causation" to offer constructive methodology. The gap is that it focused on graphical tools without covering the design-based identification strategies (DiD, IV, RDD) that the credibility revolution had already established in economics, nor did it address the institutional and cultural barriers to causal reasoning that Grosz, Rohrer, and Thoemmes (2020) would subsequently document.

> Lederer, David J., et al. "Control of confounding and reporting of results in causal inference studies: Guidance for authors from editors of respiratory, sleep, and critical care journals." Annals of the American Thoracic Society 16.1 (2019): 22-28.

Responding to the growing recognition that causal claims from observational data require methodological transparency, Lederer and a consortium of journal editors in respiratory, sleep, and critical care medicine issued the first editor-endorsed guidance on confounding control and result reporting for causal inference studies in clinical research. The guidelines require authors to specify their causal question, justify covariate selection using domain knowledge (ideally via DAGs), and distinguish clearly between causal and associational interpretations. This paper advances beyond Hernán's (2018) call to action by translating the critique into enforceable editorial policy, representing the institutional response to the causal language problem in health research. The gap relative to our work is that such editorial guidance does not yet exist in any SE venue: the ACM SIGSOFT Empirical Standards (Ralph et al., 2020) do not address causal identification assumptions, and no SE journal has adopted comparable reporting requirements for observational studies making causal claims.

> Grosz, Michael P., Julia M. Rohrer, and Felix Thoemmes. "The taboo against explicit causal inference in nonexperimental psychology." Perspectives on Psychological Science 15.5 (2020): 1243-1255.

Building on Hernán's (2018) diagnosis in epidemiology and Rohrer's (2018) introduction of causal tools, Grosz, Rohrer, and Thoemmes systematically documented the "taboo" against explicit causal inference in nonexperimental psychology---a norm that discourages researchers from stating causal questions, articulating identification assumptions, or using causal language when analyzing observational data. They showed that this taboo does not prevent researchers from making causal interpretations; it merely pushes them underground, resulting in implicit causal claims that escape methodological scrutiny. The paper identifies four harms of the taboo: it impairs study design and analysis, holds back cumulative research, creates a disconnect between findings and their downstream interpretation, and limits policy relevance. This diagnosis is strikingly parallel to the SE situation documented in LR1: the SE community similarly avoids explicit causal reasoning in observational studies while implicitly interpreting regression coefficients causally (as Berger et al., 2019, demonstrated for the PL-defect debate). The gap is that the paper is diagnostic rather than prescriptive---it calls for causal reasoning to be "brought out of the closet" but does not provide a structured framework for doing so.

> Lundberg, Ian, Rebecca Johnson, and Brandon M. Stewart. "What is your estimand? Defining the target quantity connects statistical evidence to theory." American Sociological Review 86.3 (2021): 532-565.

Lundberg, Johnson, and Stewart formalized a principle that underlies much of the reform movement across disciplines: every quantitative study should begin by clearly defining its estimand---the target quantity of interest---in precise terms that exist outside any particular statistical model. They argued that the dominant practice in sociology of defining research goals as regression coefficients within specific models prevents readers from evaluating whether the chosen procedures are appropriate and disconnects statistical evidence from substantive theory. The paper proposes a three-step workflow: set a theoretical estimand, link it to an empirical estimand via identification assumptions, and then learn from data. This estimand-first principle is directly relevant to our four-step framework---particularly Step 2 (define the causal estimand and identify assumptions)---and addresses a pervasive problem in SE: empirical studies routinely report regression coefficients without specifying what causal or descriptive quantity they target. The gap is the sociology-specific framing; translating the estimand-first principle to SE requires domain-specific examples of well-defined and poorly-defined estimands.

> Tennant, Peter W. G., et al. "Use of directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to identify confounders in applied health research: Review and recommendations." International Journal of Epidemiology 50.2 (2021): 620-632.

Tennant and colleagues conducted a systematic review of 234 applied health research articles published between 1999 and 2017 that used or mentioned directed acyclic graphs, providing the first empirical snapshot of how DAGs are actually used in practice. They found that only 21% of articles reported their target estimands, about 48% reported the adjustment sets implied by their DAGs, and two-thirds made their DAGs available---revealing a substantial gap between the theoretical promise of DAGs and their practical implementation. This pattern parallels the SE situation documented by Siebert (2023), where adoption of causal graphical methods is recent, fragmented, and often superficial. The gap is that Tennant et al. assessed DAG reporting quality without examining whether the underlying identification strategies were sound, and their recommendations focus on transparency rather than the broader question of which identification strategy to employ.

> Haber, Noah A., et al. "Causal and associational language in observational health research: A systematic evaluation." American Journal of Epidemiology 191.12 (2022): 2084-2097.

Where Hernán (2018) argued on theoretical grounds that causal euphemisms are harmful and Grosz et al. (2020) documented the taboo qualitatively, Haber and colleagues provided the first large-scale empirical evidence of the problem, systematically evaluating causal and associational language in 1,170 articles from 18 high-profile health journals published between 2010 and 2019. They found that 52% of abstracts implied moderate or strong causality through their linking language, that the most common word "associate" was rated as carrying at least some causal implication by over half of reviewers, and that action recommendations implied stronger causality than linking sentences in 45% of articles. This study demonstrates empirically that the strategy of avoiding causal language does not prevent causal interpretation---it merely makes the causal reasoning implicit and unexaminable. The finding is directly relevant to our work because SE research exhibits the same pattern: Ray et al. (2014) hedged with associational language while downstream citations and press coverage interpreted the findings causally (as our Section 5.2 citation analysis documents). The gap is that Haber et al. diagnose the language problem without proposing a solution beyond "be more explicit about causal intent."

> Wysocki, Anna C., Katherine M. Lawson, and Mijke Rhemtulla. "Statistical control requires causal justification." Advances in Methods and Practices in Psychological Science 5.2 (2022): 25152459221095823.

Formalizing the warnings that Rohrer (2018) had introduced via DAG-based reasoning, Wysocki, Lawson, and Rhemtulla demonstrated mathematically and through simulation that controlling for inappropriate variables in regression---a near-universal practice in psychology---can produce estimates more biased than uncontrolled estimates. They show that controlling for mediators, colliders, or descendants of colliders can induce or amplify confounding, and that researchers must propose and defend a causal structure before selecting control variables. This paper advances the reform movement from "think about causation" to "your specific analytical choices require causal justification," making the critique concrete and actionable. The parallel to SE is direct: large-scale observational SE studies routinely include control variables (project size, team size, commit frequency) without DAG-based justification for why those variables should be conditioned on, risking the same biases that Wysocki et al. document. The gap is the psychology-specific examples; SE researchers need analogous demonstrations with SE-relevant confounding structures.

> Killingsworth, Matthew A., Daniel Kahneman, and Barbara Mellers. "Income and emotional well-being: A conflict resolved." Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences 120.10 (2023): e2208661120.

This adversarial collaboration between Killingsworth and Kahneman---whose earlier studies had reached contradictory conclusions about whether happiness plateaus at approximately $75,000 income---provides a high-profile case study of how conflicting observational findings can be partially reconciled through more sophisticated analysis (quantile regression revealing heterogeneous effects) while leaving the fundamental causal question unresolved. They found that for the happiest 80% of people, well-being increases linearly with log income, while for the least happy 20%, the relationship flattens above approximately $100,000. The paper is relevant as a parallel to the PL-defect debate: both involve observational claims about a treatment (income, programming language) and an outcome (happiness, defects) where the core causal question---does X cause Y?---cannot be answered by increasingly sophisticated correlational analyses alone. Rohrer and Wenz (2024) would subsequently demonstrate that this paper's causal assumptions are unjustified, mirroring how Berger et al. (2019) exposed the fragility of Ray et al.'s claims in SE.

> Rohrer, Julia M. "Causal inference for psychologists who think that causal inference is not for them." Social and Personality Psychology Compass 18.3 (2024): e12948.

Six years after her foundational DAG tutorial (2018) and four years after co-documenting the taboo (Grosz et al., 2020), Rohrer produced the most pedagogically refined articulation of causal inference for a methodologically hesitant audience, arguing that causal reasoning is unavoidable even for researchers who work exclusively with observational data. The paper covers the potential outcomes framework, directed acyclic graphs, and key threats to causal interpretation (confounding, collider bias, selection bias) using examples from personality and social psychology. This paper is the closest existing analog to what our tutorial aims to achieve for SE: it demonstrates that causal inference concepts can be communicated effectively to an audience trained to believe that causal claims require experimental manipulation. The gap is that it addresses psychology-specific concerns---post-treatment bias, incremental validity tests---and does not cover the design-based identification strategies (DiD, IV, RDD, panel fixed effects) or the SE-specific confounding structures that our tutorial must address.

> Rohrer, Julia M., and Sebastian Wenz. "Inappropriate causal assumptions underlie Killingsworth, Kahneman, and Mellers' conclusions." Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences 121.46 (2024): e2408672121.

In this commentary on Killingsworth et al. (2023), Rohrer and Wenz demonstrated concretely how the failure to make causal assumptions explicit leads to unjustified causal conclusions---the very harm that Grosz et al. (2020) had warned about in the abstract. They showed that the adversarial collaboration's conclusions about income "causing" changes in well-being rest on untested and implausible identification assumptions, including the absence of time-varying confounders and reverse causality. This exchange is relevant as a worked example of the causal assessment process applied to a high-profile debate: Rohrer and Wenz effectively performed Steps 1-4 of our framework (derive causal theory, define estimand, acknowledge limitations, navigate alternatives) on a study that had not performed them, revealing that the study's apparent resolution of a long-standing conflict was illusory. The parallel to our Section 5 (applying the four-step assessment to Ray et al.) is direct and instructive. The gap is that this critique was specific to one study and did not generalize the assessment methodology into a reusable framework.

### Summary of Existing Research

Psychology and health research have grappled with a causal inference crisis that closely parallels the one we identify in software engineering. The crisis has unfolded in three phases. First, researchers diagnosed the problem: Hernán (2018) argued in epidemiology that replacing causal language with associational euphemisms does not improve inference and actively harms science, while Grosz, Rohrer, and Thoemmes (2020) documented a "taboo" in psychology that suppresses explicit causal reasoning without preventing implicit causal interpretation. Haber et al. (2022) provided large-scale empirical evidence that this taboo fails on its own terms, finding that over half of observational health studies imply moderate or strong causality despite ostensibly associational language. Second, methodological reformers introduced constructive tools: Rohrer (2018) brought graphical causal models to psychology, Wysocki et al. (2022) demonstrated that routine statistical control practices require causal justification, Lundberg et al. (2021) formalized the estimand-first principle for sociology, and Tennant et al. (2021) assessed the still-nascent adoption of DAGs in health research. Institutional responses also emerged, with clinical journal editors issuing formal guidance on confounding control (Lederer et al., 2019). Third, the consequences of ignoring causal reasoning were illustrated through high-profile case studies: Killingsworth, Kahneman, and Mellers (2023) appeared to resolve the income-happiness debate through sophisticated observational analysis, only for Rohrer and Wenz (2024) to show that the resolution rested on inappropriate causal assumptions---a sequence that directly mirrors the Ray et al. (2014) to Berger et al. (2019) trajectory in SE. Rohrer's (2024) tutorial for psychologists represents the most fully realized effort to make causal inference accessible to a methodologically hesitant empirical discipline, and serves as the closest existing analog to what our tutorial aims to accomplish for SE. The key insight from this cross-disciplinary review is that the SE community's causal inference gap is not unique but part of a broader pattern across observational empirical sciences; the solutions emerging in psychology and epidemiology---explicit causal questions, DAG-based covariate justification, estimand-first workflows, sensitivity analysis, and editorial enforcement---can inform and accelerate the SE community's own methodological reform.
